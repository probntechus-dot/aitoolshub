Month 2: The AI Stack I Built

I didn't do this all at once. I added one tool per week for the first month of transition. Here's what I landed on:

ChatGPT Plus ($20/month) ‚Äî Handles 60% of the time savings

I use ChatGPT for everything writing-adjacent. The key insight I had around week 3: I should be briefing, not writing. My job is to think deeply about what needs to be communicated, give ChatGPT that context, and then edit the output.

Time saved just on email: 3.5 hours/week. I went from writing 25-30 emails from scratch to writing 3-4 emails that genuinely required my unique perspective, and briefing ChatGPT on the rest.

Otter.ai Pro ($17/month) ‚Äî Meeting notes solved permanently

Otter joins every Zoom call. I stopped taking notes entirely. After each meeting, I spend 5 minutes reviewing Otter's AI summary and approving or tweaking action items.

Time saved: 2+ hours/week. But the hidden benefit is that I'm actually present in meetings now. No more split-attention between listening and writing. I'm fully engaged, which means better meetings and better relationships.

Zapier Professional ($49/month) ‚Äî The invisible time machine

I have 23 active Zaps. Each one is a tiny employee that never sleeps or makes mistakes. The hard-to-quantify truth is that Zapier saves me not just time but attention ‚Äî I never have to remember to do things that used to require memory.

My most valuable Zaps: lead follow-up sequences, weekly report generation, invoice reminders, and new client onboarding triggers.

Time saved: 4+ hours/week across all workflows.

Notion AI ($10/month) ‚Äî Research recall that's actually magical

I mentioned in my Notion AI review that the workspace Q&A feature is genuinely remarkable. For my client work specifically, being able to ask "what did we discuss about [topic] in the last 6 months" saves me hours of searching through old notes.

Time saved: 1.5 hours/week (more in research-heavy weeks).

Grammarly Business ($15/month) ‚Äî Quality control, always on

This one doesn't save time as much as it prevents the time cost of sending something with errors. I've caught embarrassing mistakes in client emails. It's more insurance than time-saver, but I include it because for a 6-person team, it's essential.

Month 3: The Optimized Workflow

By month three, the tools were all running smoothly and I'd figured out how to use them together. Here's my actual typical day:

Morning (7:00 AM - 9:00 AM)

  • 7:00 AM: Weekly AI digest emails from Zapier (2 minutes to read the summaries)
  • 7:05 AM: Review Otter.ai summaries from previous day's calls (5 minutes)
  • 7:15 AM: Process email with ChatGPT assistance (45 minutes instead of 90)
  • 8:00 AM: Actual strategic work ‚Äî this is my peak focus time

Midday (12:00 PM - 1:30 PM)

  • Use ChatGPT to draft any proposals or client deliverables due this week
  • Batch-create social content for the week (30 minutes instead of 2 hours)
  • Review any Zapier notifications that need attention

End of Day (5:00 PM - 6:00 PM)

  • Review action items Otter captured from today's meetings
  • Clear remaining emails with AI assistance
  • Brief notes in Notion about key decisions and priorities for tomorrow

Old me would have been at that desk until 8 PM. New me is done at 6 PM with everything handled and nothing hanging over me.

What Didn't Work (Important Stuff)

I tried a few things that didn't make the cut:

AI for strategic planning: I tried using AI to drive strategy decisions and it was actually counterproductive. AI generates confident-sounding plans but lacks the contextual wisdom that comes from really knowing your business, clients, and market. I use AI to challenge my thinking, but never to decide.

Automated client communication: I set up AI to handle some client emails automatically and had to shut it down after 3 weeks. The quality wasn't bad, but two clients noticed the "robotic" feel and mentioned it. Human relationships need human communication ‚Äî at least for key touchpoints.

AI for financial analysis: I tried having AI analyze my P&L and draw conclusions. The math it did was correct, but the insights it drew were sometimes directionally wrong because it lacked industry context. I use it to help format and structure financial reviews, not to draw strategic conclusions.
