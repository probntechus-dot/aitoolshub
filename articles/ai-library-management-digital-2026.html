AI Library Management and Digital Archive Tools

By Sarah Mitchell March 16, 2026 10 min read

The head librarian at a mid-sized university library told me something that stuck: "We have 2.3 million items in our collection. Until last year, about 400,000 of them were essentially invisible — poor metadata, inconsistent cataloging from different eras, physical items that had never been properly described. Our two catalogers would have needed 40 years to fix it manually. The AI did it in 11 weeks." That's not a marketing claim — that's the reality of what AI is now doing in libraries and archives worldwide.

Libraries and archives are having their AI moment, and it's more profound than most people outside the field realize. From the Library of Congress running AI pilots on its 170 million item collection to small-town public libraries using chatbot reference tools, the technology is touching every part of the profession. The global library management systems market is projected to reach $4.2 billion by 2027, with AI-enhanced platforms commanding 60% market share.

Where AI Is Making the Biggest Impact

Library AI applications cluster around several high-value problem areas:

  • Automated cataloging: AI reads documents, images, and audiovisual materials and generates MARC records or Dublin Core metadata automatically
  • Collection development: Predictive analytics that recommend acquisitions based on circulation patterns, ILL requests, and community demographics
  • Patron recommendation engines: "Readers advisory" AI that suggests books based on reading history, not just genre
  • Reference services: AI chatbots that handle routine reference questions, freeing librarians for complex research support
  • Digital preservation: AI that identifies at-risk digital formats and prioritizes migration workflows
  • Handwritten document transcription: HTR (Handwritten Text Recognition) AI that can read historical manuscripts

Ex Libris Alma: The Enterprise Academic Library Platform

Ex Libris Alma, part of the Clarivate family, dominates academic library management. Its AI capabilities have matured significantly — the analytics suite now provides predictive insights on collection usage, suggesting titles for deselection based on circulation patterns, cost-per-use data, and overlap analysis across the collection. For collection development librarians, this is transformative: instead of relying on gut instinct and manual spreadsheet analysis, they get AI recommendations with supporting data.

The platform's integration with Ex Libris Primo (discovery layer) creates a recommendation engine that learns from patron behavior — what people search for, what they borrow, what they return without finishing. The AI then surfaces relevant items from the collection that users might not have found through traditional search. University libraries using this feature report 15-23% increases in discovery of lesser-circulated materials.

Pricing is enterprise-level and opaque — typically $40,000-$200,000+ annually depending on institution size — but this is the system of record for most research universities worldwide.

Transkribus: Unlocking Handwritten Historical Archives

Transkribus is genuinely one of the most exciting AI tools in the cultural heritage sector. Developed at the University of Innsbruck and now operated by READ-COOP, it uses Handwritten Text Recognition (HTR) models trained on historical manuscripts to transcribe handwritten documents with remarkable accuracy.

The process works by training a model on a sample of manually transcribed pages from a specific archive collection, then applying that model to the rest of the collection. For documents in common hands (19th century German script, for example), pre-trained models can achieve 95%+ accuracy without custom training. For unusual scripts or languages, a few hundred pages of manual transcription is enough to train a usable model.

At €0 for basic use (up to 500 pages/month free) up to €299/month for high-volume institutional use, Transkribus has made HTR accessible to archives of all sizes. The Vatican Library used Transkribus to transcribe historical papal documents. The National Archives of the UK runs it on 19th-century census records. Small county historical societies use it for local church records. It's a genuinely democratizing technology.

AI Reference Services: Chatbots in the Library

The idea of replacing reference librarians with chatbots raises understandable anxiety in the profession. The reality is more nuanced — and more positive. AI reference chatbots handle the questions that are already answerable without human judgment: library hours, how to renew a book, where the bathrooms are, how to access databases from off-campus. These questions represent 60-70% of reference desk interactions at most libraries.

When AI handles routine queries, librarians can spend more time on the complex research assistance that requires genuine expertise — helping a graduate student design a systematic review methodology, assisting a community member with legal research, or providing the kind of personalized readers advisory that algorithms still can't match.

LibAnswers from Springshare (pricing based on institution size) is the market leader in library chat software and has integrated AI capabilities that learn from past reference interactions. OCLC's QuestionPoint and many public library consortia are building shared AI knowledge bases so that a question answered well for one library can benefit all libraries in the consortium.

Digital Preservation: AI for Long-Term Stewardship

Digital preservation is a crisis hiding in plain sight. Libraries and archives collectively hold millions of digital files in formats that are already obsolete or at risk — WordPerfect documents, early PDF versions, proprietary image formats from discontinued software. AI is now being used to:

  • Automatically identify file formats and flag those at risk of obsolescence
  • Prioritize migration workflows based on research value and access frequency
  • Quality-check migrated files for integrity
  • Generate technical metadata from file analysis
  • Detect duplicate files that waste storage and complicate management

Preservica (enterprise digital preservation platform) and Archivematica (open source) both have incorporated AI features for format identification and risk assessment. The Library of Congress's DPLA (Digital Public Library of America) uses AI to continuously monitor its 50 million+ digital objects for preservation risks.

Collection Development AI: Smarter Acquisitions

Libraries spend millions on materials every year, and the decision of what to buy has traditionally relied heavily on librarian expertise and vendor recommendations. AI is adding a data layer that makes these decisions much more defensible.

GOBI (Yankee Book Peddler's platform, now under EBSCO) uses usage data, ILL patterns, faculty reading lists, and curriculum data to suggest acquisitions. Libraries using predictive analytics for collection development report reducing low-use acquisitions by 20-35% while improving satisfaction among active users — they're buying less, but they're buying better.

The tension here is legitimate: AI-driven collection development can inadvertently reinforce existing biases in a collection, recommending more of what's already popular and less of underrepresented voices or emerging fields. Thoughtful librarians use AI as one input, not the deciding factor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will AI replace librarians?

The evidence so far suggests AI augments rather than replaces librarians. Routine tasks — cataloging, answering basic questions, pulling circulation statistics — are increasingly automated, freeing librarians for higher-value work. Reference services, collection development judgment, community programming, and information literacy instruction all require human expertise that AI cannot replicate. The profession is evolving, not disappearing.

What's the best AI tool for a small public library with a limited budget?

Koha (open source ILS) with AI-enhanced plugins is the best starting point — the software is free, with costs limited to implementation and hosting. For cataloging assistance, OCLC's WorldShare cataloging tools can dramatically reduce original cataloging time even for small libraries. BiblioCommons offers patron-facing discovery and recommendation tools with pricing structured for smaller institutions.

How accurate is AI handwriting recognition for historical documents?

Accuracy varies by document type and language. For 19th-20th century documents in common scripts, Transkribus and similar tools achieve 90-97% character accuracy with pre-trained models. For older or more unusual scripts, custom model training on 200-500 manually transcribed pages typically yields 85-93% accuracy. Even 85% accuracy dramatically reduces the manual transcription burden — correcting an AI draft is much faster than transcribing from scratch.

Can small archives use AI tools without dedicated IT staff?

Yes, increasingly so. Transkribus has a cloud-based interface designed for non-technical users. ArchivesSpace (archive management software) has simplified significantly. Many vendors offer managed services where the technical infrastructure is handled by the vendor. The realistic requirement is staff time to learn the tools and commitment to consistent workflows — not advanced IT expertise.
