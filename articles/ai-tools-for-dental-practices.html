7 AI Tools Making Dental Practices More Efficient

I recently sat down with a dentist who runs a three-chair practice in suburban Virginia. She told me something that stuck with me: "I went to dental school to help patients, but most days I feel like I'm running an insurance company that happens to clean teeth."

That tension — between clinical care and administrative overhead — is the defining challenge for dental practices in 2026. The average practice spends roughly 30-40% of revenue on overhead, and a huge chunk of that goes toward billing, scheduling, patient communication, and documentation.

AI tools are starting to chip away at that overhead. Some focus on diagnostics (making dentists better at catching problems early). Others target the operational side (scheduling, insurance claims, patient engagement). Here are 7 that are making a real difference.

Diagnostic AI — Catching What the Human Eye Misses

1. Pearl — FDA-Cleared AI for Dental X-Ray Analysis

Pearl is the first thing that comes up in any conversation about AI in dentistry, and for good reason. Their Second Opinion® software analyzes dental X-rays in real time, highlighting potential cavities, bone loss, calculus, and other pathologies that might be easy to miss during a busy day.

Here's what makes Pearl different from generic "AI image analysis": it's FDA-cleared as a clinical decision support tool. That means it's gone through rigorous testing and validation, and dentists can actually reference it during patient consultations without legal concerns.

A dentist I spoke with said Pearl catches about 10-15% more pathology than manual review alone — not because the dentists are bad, but because reviewing 30+ X-rays a day while managing patients, staff, and insurance creates natural fatigue. The AI doesn't get tired at 3 PM.

Best for: Any practice wanting to improve diagnostic accuracy.
Key features: Real-time X-ray analysis, pathology detection, patient visualization tools.
Pricing: Approximately $300-500/month per location.

2. Overjet — AI That Helps With Treatment Plans AND Insurance

Overjet takes dental AI in two directions simultaneously: clinical decision support and insurance workflow optimization. Their AI analyzes X-rays to detect and measure bone loss, cavities, and existing restorations — and then uses those measurements to support insurance claims.

This dual approach is clever because one of the biggest pain points in dentistry is claim denials. When an insurance company pushes back on a treatment plan, having AI-measured, visually annotated evidence makes it much harder for them to say no. Some practices using Overjet report a 20-30% reduction in claim denials.

Overjet also works on the payer side — insurance companies use their technology to review claims — which means the same AI standard is being applied on both sides of the transaction. That alignment actually speeds up approvals.

Best for: Practices dealing with high insurance claim volumes.
Key features: Bone loss measurement, caries detection, insurance-grade documentation.
Pricing: Custom pricing based on practice size.

Practice Management & Operations

3. Archy — The All-in-One AI Practice Management System

Archy is trying to be the "everything platform" for dental practices — and they're getting surprisingly close. It combines practice management, patient communication, scheduling, billing, imaging, and AI analytics into a single cloud-based system.

What caught my attention is how Archy uses AI across the entire patient journey. The scheduling AI optimizes chair time by predicting appointment durations based on procedure type and patient history. The billing module automates claim submission and follows up on unpaid claims automatically. The patient communication system sends appointment reminders, treatment plan follow-ups, and recare notices without front desk staff lifting a finger.

For practices using separate systems for scheduling, imaging, billing, and patient communication, consolidating into Archy can save both money and sanity. One practice owner told me switching to Archy eliminated three separate software subscriptions and reduced his monthly tech spend by about $400.

Best for: Practices wanting to consolidate multiple systems.
Key features: AI scheduling, automated billing, patient communication, cloud imaging.
Pricing: Starting around $500/month for a single location.

4. Dentistry.AI — Smart Patient Communication

Dentistry.AI focuses on a specific problem that costs dental practices real money: missed appointments and unfilled schedules. Their AI handles patient communication across text, email, and phone — confirming appointments, managing cancellations, filling last-minute openings, and following up on unscheduled treatment.

The standout feature is their AI phone agent. When patients call after hours (or when the front desk is slammed), the AI answers, schedules appointments, and answers common questions. It doesn't sound like a robot reading a script — it handles natural conversation flow and can transfer to a human when needed.

For practices that lose $1,000+ per unfilled chair hour, keeping that schedule full is worth far more than the monthly subscription cost. Several practices using Dentistry.AI report 15-25% fewer no-shows and a meaningful increase in production from filling cancellation slots.

Best for: Practices struggling with no-shows and scheduling gaps.
Key features: AI phone answering, automated reminders, cancellation fill, recall management.
Pricing: Varies by feature set; typically $200-400/month.

5. Curve Dental — Cloud-First Practice Management

Curve Dental has been a cloud-based practice management pioneer since before "cloud" was a buzzword in dentistry. Their platform handles scheduling, charting, imaging, billing, and patient engagement — all accessible from any browser, no server room required.

The AI features are practical rather than flashy: intelligent scheduling that reduces gaps, automated insurance eligibility verification, and smart analytics that show you exactly where your practice is losing money. Their reporting dashboard is one of the best I've seen — it surfaces KPIs like production per visit, hygiene recall rates, and case acceptance percentages in a way that's actually actionable.

For practices that are still running server-based software (looking at you, Dentrix users considering a switch), Curve's cloud-native architecture means no more IT headaches, automatic updates, and access from anywhere.

Best for: Practices wanting to move away from server-based systems.
Key features: Cloud-native PMS, intelligent scheduling, automated eligibility checks.
Pricing: Starting around $350/month per location.

6. Open Dental AI — Customizable and Developer-Friendly

Open Dental has been the "open-source" option in dental software for years. Their platform is highly customizable, and the growing AI plugin ecosystem means you can add intelligence to scheduling, treatment planning, and patient communication without switching your entire system.

What makes Open Dental unique is its API-friendly architecture. If you have specific workflows or want to integrate with niche tools, Open Dental lets you build (or hire someone to build) exactly what you need. The downside? It requires more technical know-how than turnkey solutions like Archy or Curve.

The community around Open Dental is active and helpful — there's a robust forum where dental professionals share custom reports, automation scripts, and integration tips. For practices with a tech-savvy office manager (or a willingness to hire a consultant), the flexibility is unmatched.

Best for: Tech-savvy practices that want maximum customization.
Key features: Open API, plugin ecosystem, community-driven development.
Pricing: One-time license fee (~$179/month support) vs. subscription competitors.

7. Tab32 — Cloud Practice Management With Built-In Teledentistry

Tab32 stands out by combining traditional practice management with teledentistry capabilities — something that became essential during the pandemic and has stayed relevant as patients increasingly expect virtual consultation options.

Their AI features include automated patient risk assessments, smart scheduling that accounts for procedure complexity and equipment availability, and predictive analytics for patient retention. The teledentistry module lets dentists conduct virtual screenings, follow-up consultations, and emergency triage calls directly within the platform.

For multi-location practices or DSOs (Dental Service Organizations), Tab32's centralized dashboard provides a single view across all locations — production numbers, scheduling efficiency, and patient satisfaction metrics all in one place.

Best for: Multi-location practices and DSOs needing centralized management.
Key features: Teledentistry, multi-location management, patient risk analytics.
Pricing: Custom pricing based on practice size and locations.

Getting Started: A Practical Approach

If you're a dental practice owner reading this and feeling overwhelmed, here's my advice: don't try to implement everything at once.

Step 1: Start with diagnostic AI (Pearl or Overjet). The ROI is immediate — better case acceptance, fewer missed findings, and stronger insurance documentation. Most practices see payback within 2-3 months.

Step 2: Add patient communication AI (Dentistry.AI). Reducing no-shows and filling cancellation slots is the lowest-hanging fruit for increasing production without adding patients.

Step 3: Evaluate whether your practice management system needs an upgrade. If you're spending more time fighting your software than using it, platforms like Archy, Curve, or Tab32 can simplify operations significantly.

The dental practices that will thrive in the next few years aren't necessarily the ones with the fanciest equipment. They're the ones that use technology to reduce overhead, improve patient experience, and let dentists focus on what they're actually trained to do: providing excellent care.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is AI accurate enough for dental diagnostics?

Yes, and in many cases more consistent than human-only review. Studies show AI tools like Pearl and Overjet detect cavities and bone loss with 90-95% accuracy. However, they're designed to assist dentists — not replace clinical judgment. The AI flags potential issues, and the dentist makes the final call.

How much do AI dental tools cost?

Pricing varies widely. Diagnostic AI tools like Pearl and Overjet typically run $300-500/month per location. Practice management platforms like Archy or Curve Dental range from $300-700/month depending on features and practice size. Most offer demos and pilot programs.

Are AI dental tools HIPAA compliant?

All reputable AI dental tools (Pearl, Overjet, Archy, Curve Dental, Tab32) are built with HIPAA compliance as a baseline requirement. They use encrypted data storage, secure transmission protocols, and business associate agreements. Always verify compliance documentation before signing.

Can AI help with dental insurance claims?

Yes. Tools like Archy and Overjet specifically help with insurance workflows. AI-annotated X-rays provide visual evidence for treatment plans, which can reduce claim denials. Archy automates claim submission and tracking. Some practices report 20-30% fewer denied claims after implementing AI documentation.

Will AI replace dental hygienists or assistants?

No. AI handles data analysis, scheduling optimization, and documentation — tasks that support clinical staff rather than replace them. Dental hygienists and assistants perform hands-on patient care that AI cannot replicate. If anything, AI makes their jobs easier by handling paperwork and administrative burden.
