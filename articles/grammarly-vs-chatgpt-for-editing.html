Grammarly vs ChatGPT for Editing: Do You Need Both?

⏱ 8 min read
By Sarah Mitchell March 14, 2026
📝 Disclosure: Some links in this article are affiliate links. If you purchase through them, we may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. We only recommend tools we've personally tested.

I'll be honest — I spent way too long using Grammarly and ChatGPT as if they did the same thing. One afternoon I was pasting a blog post into ChatGPT asking it to "fix the grammar," then running it through Grammarly anyway, wondering why I was doubling my work.

Sound familiar? If you write anything online — blog posts, emails, social media captions, client reports — you've probably asked yourself: Do I really need both of these tools, or is one enough?

I've been using Grammarly Premium for over two years and ChatGPT Plus since it launched. After putting them through dozens of real editing tasks, I can tell you they're not interchangeable. They do very different things, and understanding those differences will save you time and money.

Let's break it down.

What Each Tool Actually Does (They're Not the Same)

Before we compare features, let's get clear on what these tools are built for — because that's where most of the confusion starts.

Grammarly is a dedicated writing assistant. It sits inside your browser, your email client, Google Docs, and Microsoft Word. Every time you type, it scans your text for grammar mistakes, spelling errors, punctuation issues, awkward phrasing, and tone inconsistencies. It works in real time, right where you write.

ChatGPT is a general-purpose AI. It can write, rewrite, brainstorm, summarize, translate, code, and answer questions. Editing is just one of dozens of things it can do. You give it text, ask it to improve something specific, and it gives you a new version.

Think of it this way: Grammarly is like a meticulous copy editor sitting on your shoulder. ChatGPT is like a brilliant colleague you can ask to rewrite your entire paragraph in a different style.

Grammar and Spelling: The Bread and Butter

This one's straightforward. Grammarly wins on grammar checking, and it's not close.

Grammarly catches errors I didn't even know I was making — misplaced commas, subject-verb agreement issues buried in long sentences, inconsistent hyphenation. It highlights them inline, explains why they're wrong, and lets me fix them with one click. That feedback loop actually teaches you to write better over time.

ChatGPT can fix grammar too, but you have to paste your text in and ask. It doesn't always explain what it changed or why. Sometimes it quietly rewrites your sentence while fixing a comma splice, and now your original meaning is slightly different. That's a problem if you're editing client work or anything where the author's voice matters.

For quick, reliable grammar fixes across everything you type? Grammarly, hands down.

Style and Clarity Suggestions

Grammarly Premium offers style suggestions — it'll flag passive voice, wordy sentences, overused words, and unclear phrasing. These suggestions are good, but they're rule-based. Sometimes it flags perfectly fine sentences as "hard to read" when they're actually well-crafted.

ChatGPT takes a completely different approach. Instead of flagging issues, it rewrites your text in whatever style you ask for. Want something more conversational? More formal? Shorter? Punchier? Just tell it. It can transform a dry corporate email into something that actually sounds like a human wrote it.

Where ChatGPT really shines is restructuring. If a paragraph is technically correct but reads like a mess, Grammarly won't catch that. ChatGPT can reorganize your ideas, improve the flow, and make the whole piece read more naturally.

Tone Detection and Adjustment

Grammarly has a built-in tone detector that reads your text and tells you how it might come across — confident, friendly, formal, worried, and so on. It's handy for emails where you're not sure if you sound too aggressive or too passive. But it's a detector, not a fixer. It tells you the problem; it doesn't rewrite the text for you.

ChatGPT doesn't detect tone the way Grammarly does, but it can change tone on command. "Make this sound warmer." "Make this more direct." "Rewrite this like I'm explaining it to a friend." That flexibility is incredibly useful, especially when you're adapting the same content for different audiences.

Plagiarism Detection

This is where Grammarly has a clear, unmatched advantage. Grammarly Premium includes a plagiarism checker that scans your text against billions of web pages and academic databases. If you're a student, a blogger, or a content manager who publishes at scale, this feature alone might justify the subscription.

ChatGPT has no plagiarism detection. None. It can't scan the web to check if your text matches existing content. And ironically, since ChatGPT generates text based on patterns in its training data, there's always a small risk that its output could closely resemble existing content somewhere online.

If plagiarism checking matters to you — and for professional writers, it should — you need Grammarly or a standalone tool like Turnitin or Copyscape.

Where You Can Use Them

Grammarly works everywhere you type. Its browser extension runs in Gmail, Google Docs, LinkedIn, Twitter, Slack, WordPress, and dozens of other platforms. There's also a desktop app, a mobile keyboard, and a Microsoft Office integration. The convenience factor is enormous — it's always on, always watching, always catching mistakes before you hit send.

ChatGPT lives in a separate window. You have to copy your text, paste it into the chat, wait for the response, then copy the edited version back. It works — but it's not seamless. The ChatGPT desktop app helps a bit, but it's nowhere near as integrated as Grammarly's ecosystem.

For quick edits on the fly, Grammarly's integration advantage is massive.

Real Use Cases: When to Use Which

Use Grammarly when:

  • Writing emails and you want real-time error catching
  • Publishing blog posts and need plagiarism checks
  • Writing in Google Docs or Word and want inline suggestions
  • English is your second language and you want consistent grammar support
  • You work in a team and need consistent brand tone (Grammarly Business)

Use ChatGPT when:

  • You need to rewrite a paragraph in a completely different style
  • You're stuck on how to phrase something and want creative options
  • You want to simplify complex or technical writing
  • You need to adapt content for different audiences
  • You're brainstorming headlines, intros, or outlines

Use both when:

  • You write and publish content professionally
  • You want to draft with ChatGPT, then polish with Grammarly
  • You need both creative flexibility and error-free output

🏆 The Verdict

These tools complement each other — they don't compete. Grammarly is your safety net for grammar, spelling, and plagiarism. ChatGPT is your creative partner for rewriting, restructuring, and tone shifting. If you can only pick one, choose Grammarly if you mostly need error-free writing, or ChatGPT if you need help with how to say things. But honestly? Using both together is the sweet spot most professional writers land on.

My Personal Workflow

Here's exactly how I use both tools together, every single day:

  1. Draft: I write my first draft in Google Docs with Grammarly's extension active. It catches typos and basic mistakes as I go.
  2. Restructure: If a section feels clunky, I paste it into ChatGPT and ask for a clearer version. Sometimes I ask for three options and pick the best one.
  3. Polish: I bring the restructured text back to Google Docs. Grammarly runs through it again for any new errors.
  4. Plagiarism check: Before publishing, I run the full article through Grammarly's plagiarism checker.
  5. Final read: I read the article out loud. No AI can replace that final human pass.

This workflow takes me about 30 minutes for a 1,500-word article. Without these tools, the same editing process used to take me over an hour.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is ChatGPT better than Grammarly for editing?

It depends on what kind of editing you need. Grammarly is better for catching grammar, spelling, and punctuation errors in real time. ChatGPT is better for rewriting, restructuring, and adjusting tone. They solve different problems.

Can ChatGPT check for plagiarism like Grammarly?

No. ChatGPT doesn't have a plagiarism detection feature. Grammarly Premium scans your text against billions of web pages and academic papers to flag potential matches.

Is Grammarly worth paying for if I already use ChatGPT?

Yes, especially if you write frequently across different platforms. Grammarly's browser extension works in real time wherever you type — email, social media, documents. That convenience is hard to replicate with ChatGPT.

Can I use Grammarly and ChatGPT together?

Absolutely. Many professional writers draft or restructure with ChatGPT, then run the final version through Grammarly for error-free, plagiarism-checked output. It's a powerful combination.

Which tool is better for academic writing?

Grammarly, because it offers plagiarism detection, formal tone suggestions, and citation-style recommendations. ChatGPT can help brainstorm or restructure arguments, but always verify any facts it generates.
