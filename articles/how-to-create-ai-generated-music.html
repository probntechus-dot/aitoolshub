How to Write Better AI Music Prompts

The quality of your AI-generated music depends heavily on how you write your prompt. Here's what I've learned from generating hundreds of tracks:

Be Specific About Genre and Sub-Genre

Don't just say "rock." Say "indie garage rock with fuzzy guitars and deadpan vocals." The more specific you get, the more the AI has to work with. Think about sub-genres, eras, and reference points.

Describe the Mood and Energy

"Happy" is vague. "Warm, nostalgic, like driving home on a summer evening" gives the AI a much clearer picture. Emotional descriptions work surprisingly well with these tools.

Mention Instruments

If you want acoustic guitar, say so. If you want synth pads, specify them. Don't assume the AI will guess the right instrumentation. Being explicit about instruments gives you way more consistent results.

Specify Tempo and Energy Arc

Tell the AI if you want something slow and building, or fast and energetic from the start. Phrases like "starts quiet and builds to a powerful chorus" help shape the song structure.

Write Your Own Lyrics (When Possible)

AI-generated lyrics are decent, but they tend to be generic. If you write your own lyrics and paste them into tools like Suno, the output feels much more personal and intentional. You don't need to be a poet — just write honestly.

💡 Example Prompts That Work Well

Prompt 1: "Chill lo-fi hip-hop beat with jazzy piano chords, soft vinyl crackle, and a mellow bass line. Relaxing study music vibes, 85 BPM."

Prompt 2: "Upbeat 80s synthwave track with pulsing arpeggios, heavy reverb drums, and a soaring lead synth. Think neon lights and fast cars."

Prompt 3: "Acoustic folk ballad with fingerpicked guitar, soft harmonica, and gentle female vocals singing about leaving a small town."

Understanding Licensing: Can You Actually Use This Music?

This is the part most people skip — and then regret later. Licensing for AI-generated music varies a lot between platforms, so let me break it down clearly.

Free Tiers: Usually Non-Commercial

Most free plans let you generate music for personal use only. You can listen to it, share it with friends, maybe use it in a non-monetized video. But you can't sell it, put it in ads, or use it in commercial content without upgrading.

Paid Plans: Commercial Rights Included

Suno Pro, Udio's paid tier, Soundraw, and Mubert's creator plans all include commercial licenses. This means you can use the music in YouTube videos (even monetized ones), podcasts, client projects, and more.

Full Copyright Ownership: Rare but Available

AIVA's Pro plan is one of the few that gives you full copyright ownership of generated tracks. Most other platforms grant you a license to use the music but retain some underlying rights. Read the fine print if this matters for your use case.

What About Spotify and Streaming?

This is a gray area. Some distributors accept AI-generated music, others don't. Spotify has stated they'll take down AI tracks that are clearly designed to game the streaming system, but original AI-assisted compositions are generally fine. If you plan to distribute on streaming platforms, check your distributor's policy first.

Step-by-Step: Create Your First AI Song

Let me walk you through making a song on Suno since it's the easiest place to start:

  1. Sign up at suno.com — Free accounts get a few generations per day
  2. Click "Create" — You'll see a text box for your prompt
  3. Write a detailed prompt — Include genre, mood, tempo, and instruments
  4. Choose "Custom" or "Simple" mode — Custom lets you paste your own lyrics
  5. Hit Generate — Suno will create two versions for you to compare
  6. Listen and pick your favorite — You can extend, remix, or regenerate
  7. Download — WAV or MP3, depending on your plan

The whole process takes about 2 minutes. I'm not exaggerating. From prompt to finished song, it's faster than making a cup of coffee.

Tips I Wish I Knew When I Started

  1. Generate multiple versions. Your first attempt is rarely the best. Create 5-10 variations and pick the winner.
  2. Mix and match tools. Use Suno for the vocals, then add AI-generated instrumentals from Soundraw underneath. Layer your tools.
  3. Don't over-prompt. Sometimes "sad acoustic ballad" works better than a 200-word prompt. Experiment with both short and long descriptions.
  4. Save everything. You can't always recreate the same track from the same prompt. If you like something, download it immediately.
  5. Use AI music as a starting point. Import AI tracks into GarageBand or Audacity and add your own touches. The combination of AI + human input usually sounds best.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use AI-generated music commercially?

It depends on the tool. Suno and Udio's paid plans grant commercial licenses. Soundraw and Mubert also offer commercial-use plans. Always check each platform's licensing terms before using tracks in videos, ads, or products.

Do I need musical experience to make AI music?

Not at all. Most AI music tools are designed for people with zero musical training. You describe what you want in plain English — the AI handles melody, harmony, instrumentation, and mixing.

Which AI music tool is best for beginners?

Suno is widely considered the most beginner-friendly option. You type a prompt, pick a style, and get a full song in under a minute. Soundraw is another great choice if you want more control over mood and structure.

Will AI-generated music get flagged on YouTube?

If you use a tool that grants you a proper license (like Suno Pro or Soundraw), your tracks should not get Content ID claims. However, free-tier generations from some platforms may have restrictions. Always download from a paid plan for YouTube content.

Can AI compose music in specific genres like jazz or EDM?

Yes. Most AI music generators let you specify genre, mood, tempo, and instruments. AIVA excels at classical and cinematic scores, while Suno and Udio handle pop, hip-hop, EDM, jazz, and dozens of other styles.
