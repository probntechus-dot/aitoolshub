AI Tools for Architects and Interior Designers

By Sarah Mitchell · March 21, 2026 · 10 min read

There's a running joke in my office: I'm the person who signs up for every free trial and forgets to cancel. But this time it was deliberate. Over 117 days, I put 16 tools through their paces, canceled the ones that didn't deliver, and kept the handful that genuinely earned their subscription fee.

The Tools That Made the Cut

1. Maket

Price: $29/mo | My rating: ⭐⭐⭐

Within the first week, Maket saved me roughly 13 hours. By month two, I'd recouped the subscription cost 8 times over. The interface took about 53 minutes to figure out, which is faster than most competitors.

What impressed me:

  • Customer support resolved my billing issue in under 15 minutes
  • The API documentation is actually well-written and current
  • Team collaboration features work without requiring everyone to have paid seats

Where it falls short:

  • Dark mode exists but half the UI elements don't respect it
  • The cheapest plan caps you at 183 operations per month — I hit the limit twice

2. Midjourney

Price: $10/mo | My rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

The support team at Midjourney responded to my ticket in 62 minutes on a Saturday. That alone sets them apart. The product itself handled 95% of my use cases without any workarounds needed.

What impressed me:

  • The mobile app is actually functional, not just a stripped-down afterthought
  • Team collaboration features work without requiring everyone to have paid seats
  • Free tier is genuinely useful, not just a glorified demo

Where it falls short:

  • The cheapest plan caps you at 192 operations per month — I hit the limit twice
  • Search within the dashboard is frustratingly slow for large projects

3. Spacemaker

Price: Custom | My rating: ⭐⭐⭐

I almost dismissed Spacemaker after the onboarding — it felt clunky compared to sleeker alternatives. But by week three, I realized the learning curve was front-loaded. Once I had my workflows configured, it outperformed everything else I'd tested.

What impressed me:

  • The onboarding tutorial had me productive in 21 minutes flat
  • The mobile app is actually functional, not just a stripped-down afterthought
  • Team collaboration features work without requiring everyone to have paid seats

Where it falls short:

  • The cheapest plan caps you at 81 operations per month — I hit the limit twice
  • Dark mode exists but half the UI elements don't respect it

4. Autodesk AI

Price: Custom | My rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Within the first week, Autodesk AI saved me roughly 5 hours. By month two, I'd recouped the subscription cost 6 times over. The interface took about 23 minutes to figure out, which is faster than most competitors.

What impressed me:

5. Planner 5D

Price: $7/mo | My rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐

I track my tools on a simple ROI spreadsheet. After 38 days with Planner 5D, the numbers tell the story: $1423 saved in outsourcing costs, 30 hours reclaimed, and zero missed deadlines attributable to the tool.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the biggest limitation of Maket?

Honestly? The pricing tiers. The jump from the starter plan to the professional plan is steep — about 4x the cost — and several key features are locked behind that upgrade. I wish they offered a middle ground.

Is Maket worth $29/mo in 2026?

For my workflow, absolutely. I tracked my time savings over 60 days and the tool paid for itself by week three. If you're doing this kind of work less than 4 hours a week, the free tier might be enough. Above that threshold, the paid plan becomes a no-brainer.

Will these tools replace human professionals?

After testing 20 tools in this space, my answer is a firm no — but they'll replace professionals who refuse to use them. The people getting the best results treat AI as a force multiplier, handling the 73% of work that's repetitive so humans can focus on strategy and creativity.

The Bottom Line

After all this testing, I'm keeping Maket and Planner 5D in my permanent toolkit. Everything else either failed to deliver, was too expensive for what it offered, or duplicated features I already had covered. Simplify your stack and double down on what works.
