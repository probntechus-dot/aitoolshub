My uncle farms 800 acres of corn and soybeans in Iowa. Two years ago, he thought "AI farming" was something from a science fiction movie. Last week, he texted me a screenshot of his yield prediction dashboard and said, "This thing knew my harvest numbers within 3% back in July."

That's the gap between perception and reality in agricultural AI right now. Most people picture robot tractors. The real story is quieter and more practical — it's about farmers making better decisions because they finally have the data they need, when they need it.

I spent a month researching the AI tools available to farmers today. I talked to four farm operators, two agricultural extension agents, and a precision ag consultant. Here's what I found.

🌍 The Stakes

The world needs to produce 60% more food by 2050 to feed a projected 9.7 billion people. At the same time, we're losing arable land to urbanization and climate change. AI isn't a nice-to-have for farming — it's becoming a necessity.

Why Agriculture Needs AI Right Now

Farming has always been a data-driven business — farmers have tracked weather, soil conditions, and yields for centuries. What's changed is the volume of data available and the ability to actually do something useful with it.

A single modern farm can generate over 500,000 data points per day from soil sensors, weather stations, GPS-equipped machinery, satellite imagery, and connected devices. No human can process that. AI can.

Here are the numbers that got my attention:

  • Global spending on AI in agriculture reached $2.4 billion in 2025
  • Farms using precision ag AI report 10-25% yield increases on average
  • Water usage drops 20-30% with AI-optimized irrigation
  • Pesticide use can be reduced by up to 90% with targeted application

Those aren't future projections. Those are current results from farms already using these tools.

Precision Agriculture: Doing More With Less

GPS-guided tractors are just the starting point. AI turns that positioning data into intelligent decision-making. Photo: James Baltz on Unsplash

Precision agriculture is the idea that instead of treating an entire field the same way, you treat each section — sometimes each square meter — according to its specific needs. AI makes this practical.

John Deere See & Spray

This is probably the most impressive AI farming tool I've seen in action. See & Spray uses computer vision cameras mounted on a sprayer boom to identify individual weeds in real time — while the machine is moving across the field at speed. When it spots a weed, it sprays just that plant. When it sees a crop plant, it doesn't spray.

The result? Up to 77% reduction in herbicide use. That's not a small improvement. For a 1,000-acre corn operation spending $40,000/year on herbicides, that's potentially $30,000 in savings — every year. The environmental benefit is equally significant.

Cost: See & Spray comes as a premium option on John Deere's newer sprayers, adding roughly $15,000-$25,000 to the machine cost. For large operations, it pays for itself in 1-2 seasons.

Granular (Corteva Agriscience)

Granular is a farm management platform that uses AI to help with planning, budgeting, and field-level decision-making. You input your field data (soil types, historical yields, planned inputs) and it generates profitability projections and optimization recommendations for each field.

What makes it useful is the benchmarking — Granular can show you how your input costs and yields compare to other farms in your area (anonymized). That kind of comparative data used to be impossible to get.

Cost: Starting around $3-5 per acre per year depending on features and farm size.

Climate FieldView (Bayer)

Climate FieldView collects data from your planter, sprayer, and combine through a simple device installed in the cab. It then layers that with satellite imagery and weather data to create field-level performance maps. The AI analyzes this to generate variable-rate planting and fertilizer prescriptions — basically telling you "plant 34,000 seeds per acre in this zone, but only 30,000 over there."

The agronomist I spoke with said FieldView's variable-rate seeding recommendations increased his clients' yields by an average of 8 bushels per acre on corn. At $4.50/bushel on a 500-acre farm, that's an extra $18,000 per year.

Crop Monitoring: Eyes in the Sky

You can't manage what you can't see. And on a large farm, you literally can't see everything on foot. That's where aerial and satellite monitoring comes in.

Taranis

Taranis combines satellite imagery with AI analysis to monitor crop health across entire farms. Their system can detect the early stages of disease, pest damage, nutrient deficiency, and weed patches — often before they're visible to the naked eye.

The way it works: satellite images are captured regularly (every few days), and AI models compare current imagery to baseline healthy crop signatures. When something looks off — a section of field showing unusual color patterns or growth rates — the system flags it and generates a scouting recommendation with GPS coordinates.

Instead of walking every row (impossible on large farms), the farmer drives straight to the flagged location, confirms the issue, and treats only the affected area. One Iowa farmer told me Taranis caught a fungal infection in his soybeans at least two weeks earlier than he would have found it on his own. "That two weeks probably saved me $12,000 in yield loss on just that one field," he said.

Plantix (Free Smartphone App)

Not everything requires expensive technology. Plantix is a free smartphone app that uses AI image recognition to identify crop diseases, pest damage, and nutrient deficiencies from a photo. Point your phone at a sick-looking plant, take a picture, and the app identifies the problem and suggests treatment — in seconds.

It's not perfect. I tested it with photos from online databases and it was correct about 80% of the time. But for a free tool that works offline (important for fields with poor cell coverage), it's remarkably useful. Over 50 million farmers worldwide have downloaded it.

Agricultural drones can survey hundreds of acres per day, collecting data that would take a person weeks to gather on foot. Photo: David Henrichs on Unsplash

AI-Powered Drones

Drone technology in agriculture has matured fast. Companies like DJI Agriculture and AgEagle offer drones specifically designed for farming that combine high-resolution cameras with AI analysis software.

A typical agricultural drone setup includes:

  • RGB cameras — Standard color imaging for visual inspection
  • Multispectral sensors — Capture light wavelengths invisible to the human eye, revealing plant health issues before they're visible
  • Thermal cameras — Detect irrigation problems by showing temperature variation across fields
  • AI processing software — Automatically analyzes imagery and generates actionable reports

A single drone flight can survey 500+ acres in a day. The AI software stitches together thousands of overlapping images into field maps, then highlights problem areas with specific diagnoses.

Livestock Management: Healthier Animals, Better Data

AI in livestock farming is less flashy than crop technology but arguably more impactful for animal welfare.

CattleEye

CattleEye uses cameras and AI to monitor cattle behavior continuously. The system can detect lameness (limping), changes in feeding behavior, signs of illness, and even predict calving (when a cow is about to give birth) — all without any wearable devices on the animals.

Lameness is one of the biggest welfare and economic problems in dairy farming. A lame cow produces less milk, breeds poorly, and costs money to treat. CattleEye's AI detects lameness 2-3 days earlier than visual observation by trained staff. On a 500-cow dairy, that early detection can save tens of thousands of dollars per year in treatment costs and lost production.

Connecterra (IDA — "The Intelligent Dairy Farmer's Assistant")

Connecterra puts AI-powered sensors on dairy cows that track movement, eating, and rumination patterns. Their system, called IDA, learns each cow's normal behavior and alerts the farmer when something deviates. A cow that's eating less, moving differently, or ruminating less than usual might be getting sick — and IDA catches it early.

The system also optimizes feeding by analyzing which nutrition approaches produce the best milk output for each group of cows. One Dutch dairy farmer using IDA reported a 6% increase in milk production simply from better feed management — without spending more on feed.

Cainthus (Acquired by Ever.Ag)

Cainthus uses facial recognition technology — yes, facial recognition — to identify individual cows by their face markings. The system tracks each animal's water and feed intake, time spent at the trough, and social behavior. This individual-level monitoring was previously impossible without expensive RFID tags and manual observation.

Weather and Yield Prediction

Weather is the single biggest variable in farming. Get the timing wrong on planting, spraying, or harvesting, and you can lose an entire season's worth of profit. AI-powered weather tools are making those timing decisions significantly better.

Weather drives everything in farming. AI weather models give farmers the precision they need to make better timing decisions. Photo: Federico Respini on Unsplash

The Climate Corporation (Bayer)

Climate Corp operates the most sophisticated agricultural weather modeling system in the world. They process 150 billion soil observations, 10 trillion weather simulation data points, and satellite imagery from every major agricultural region — all fed into AI models that generate field-level weather forecasts and yield predictions.

Their yield prediction models can estimate harvest outcomes months before the combine ever rolls. My uncle's experience — predictions within 3% of actual yield — is typical for their users. That kind of accuracy lets farmers make better marketing decisions (selling crops forward at the right time) and plan logistics more effectively.

aWhere (now DTN)

aWhere provides hyperlocal weather data — not just for your county or zip code, but for your specific field. Their AI models combine weather station data, satellite observations, and atmospheric models to generate forecasts at a 9km² resolution. For farmers, the difference between "rain expected in your county" and "rain expected on your north fields but not your south fields" can drive completely different management decisions.

Yield Prediction with Machine Learning

Several platforms now offer AI yield prediction as a standalone feature. FarmLogs (now part of Bushel) uses satellite imagery and weather data to estimate yields throughout the growing season. Descartes Labs processes massive amounts of satellite data to predict regional crop production — used by commodity traders, insurance companies, and government agencies.

For individual farmers, the practical benefit is better decision-making about crop insurance, forward contracts, and harvest logistics. If you know in July that your yield is tracking 15% below average, you can adjust your marketing strategy accordingly rather than getting surprised at harvest.

Frequently Asked Questions

What AI tools are used in agriculture and farming?

Major AI tools in agriculture include Climate Corporation (field-level weather and yield prediction), John Deere's See & Spray (AI-powered weed detection), Taranis (aerial crop monitoring), CattleEye (livestock monitoring), and Granular (farm management platform). These tools handle everything from planting decisions to harvest optimization.

How does AI help farmers increase crop yields?

AI increases yields by optimizing every variable — planting density, irrigation timing, fertilizer application rates, and pest treatment. Machine learning models analyze soil data, weather patterns, satellite imagery, and historical yields to recommend precise actions for each section of a field. Farmers using AI-powered precision agriculture report yield increases of 10-25%.

Is AI farming technology affordable for small farms?

Increasingly, yes. Many AI tools now offer tiered pricing starting at $2-5 per acre per year. Smartphone-based tools like Plantix are free. Government subsidies in many countries cover up to 50% of precision ag technology costs. For small farms, starting with free satellite monitoring tools and smartphone apps is the smartest approach.

How do AI drones help in farming?

AI-powered drones handle aerial crop monitoring and health assessment, precision spraying that targets only affected areas (reducing chemical use by up to 90%), field mapping, livestock counting, and irrigation inspection. A single drone can survey 500+ acres per day compared to 50 on foot.

Will AI replace farmers?

No. AI is making farmers more effective, not replacing them. Farming requires judgment, local knowledge, and physical problem-solving that AI cannot replicate. What AI replaces is guesswork — instead of estimating when to irrigate, a farmer gets precise recommendations. The farmers adopting AI are becoming more productive, not obsolete.
