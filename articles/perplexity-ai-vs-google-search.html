I Used Perplexity AI Instead of Google for a Week. Here's What Happened

⏱ 12 min read
Sarah Mitchell AI Tools Reviewer & Tech Writer
· 📅 March 14, 2026 · ⏱ 12 min read
🐦 Tweet in Share 📘 Share
📝 Editorial note: This is a personal experience article. I wasn't paid by Perplexity or Google. I just wanted to see if the hype was real. Spoiler: it's complicated.

Last Monday, I did something I never thought I'd do. I changed my default browser search engine from Google—which I've used daily since roughly 2004—to Perplexity AI.

The rules were simple: every search I'd normally do on Google, I'd do on Perplexity instead. Restaurant recommendations, product research, coding questions, random "what year did X happen" queries, all of it. Seven full days.

I kept a journal. I tracked how many times I caved and went back to Google (more than I expected). And by the end of the week, I had a pretty clear picture of where AI search is genuinely better—and where Google still can't be touched.

Here's the full, unfiltered account.

Day 1 (Monday): The Honeymoon Phase

My first search of the day was "best noise-canceling headphones under $200 in 2026." On Google, this query gives you what it always gives you—a wall of sponsored results, followed by SEO-optimized listicles from Wirecutter, Tom's Guide, and RTINGS. You click through 3-4 articles, compare recommendations, and piece together an answer yourself.

Perplexity? It gave me a single, well-organized answer. Five headphones, ranked. Each one with the current price, where to buy it, and a one-sentence summary of the pros and cons. Sources cited at the bottom—including RTINGS, Wirecutter, and a Reddit thread from r/headphones I wouldn't have found on my own.

Time spent on Google for this type of query: about 8-10 minutes of clicking around. Time on Perplexity: maybe 45 seconds to read the answer.

I was hooked. "Why have I been using Google like a caveman?" I thought.

Day 2 (Tuesday): The Cracks Start Showing

Tuesday morning I needed to find a nearby Thai restaurant that was open for lunch. I typed "thai restaurant near me open now" into Perplexity and got... a list of highly-rated Thai restaurants in my general area. But here's the thing—no map. No hours displayed in real-time. No "open now" filter. No directions.

I immediately opened Google Maps. Took about 4 seconds to find exactly what I needed.

That was the first time I broke my own rule. And it wouldn't be the last.

Later that day I tried shopping. "Canon EOS R6 Mark III price" on Perplexity gave me a paragraph about the camera's retail price ($2,499 MSRP) and some commentary on where to find deals. But Google Shopping showed me 15 different retailers with real-time prices, stock availability, and shipping estimates. Perplexity couldn't touch that.

Lesson learned: Perplexity is an answer engine. Google is an everything engine. They're playing different games.

Day 3 (Wednesday): Where Perplexity Actually Shines

Wednesday is when things got interesting. I was researching a topic for an article—specifically, I wanted to understand the current state of nuclear fusion energy in 2026. This is exactly the kind of complex, multi-faceted question where Google's traditional approach falls apart.

On Google, you'd search "nuclear fusion 2026 progress," get a bunch of news articles from different dates, open 6-7 tabs, cross-reference them, and try to build a coherent picture yourself. It works, but it takes 30-40 minutes.

Perplexity synthesized information from 12 sources—academic papers, news articles, government reports—into a single, comprehensive answer. It covered the NIF's latest experiments, Commonwealth Fusion Systems' progress, the ITER timeline update, and private funding trends. All in about 800 words with numbered citations.

I asked a follow-up: "Which private companies are closest to commercial viability?" It remembered the context and gave me a focused answer on Helion Energy (projected 2028 pilot), TAE Technologies, and Commonwealth Fusion Systems.

This is where the "AI search" paradigm actually works. For deep research questions where you need synthesized understanding—not just links—Perplexity is significantly faster and often more useful than Google.

Day 4 (Thursday): The Accuracy Test

I spent Thursday deliberately trying to catch Perplexity in mistakes. I asked it 20 factual questions—things like population figures, historical dates, scientific facts, and recent news events.

Results: 17 out of 20 were accurate and well-sourced. The three misses were interesting:

  • A slightly outdated statistic — It cited a 2025 report for a figure that had been updated in February 2026
  • A misinterpreted source — It said a company's revenue was $4.2 billion when the source actually said $4.2 billion in projected revenue
  • A subtle factual error — It attributed a quote to the wrong person (confused two similarly named researchers)

Now here's the key difference: because Perplexity shows its sources, I could spot these errors and verify them. With Google's AI Overviews—that box at the top of search results—I've seen similar mistakes, but the sources are harder to check because Google doesn't always cite clearly.

85% accuracy with verifiable sources beats 90% accuracy with no way to check, in my book.

Day 5 (Friday): The Productivity Day

Friday was a heavy work day, and I tracked every search I did. Final count: 47 searches.

Here's how they broke down:

  • Research/knowledge queries: 18 (Perplexity won 16 of these)
  • Navigation/local: 8 (Google won all 8)
  • Shopping/price checks: 6 (Google won 5, Perplexity won 1)
  • Quick facts: 9 (Tie—both answered instantly)
  • How-to/tutorials: 6 (Perplexity won 4, Google won 2—YouTube was the difference)

So Perplexity "won" about 21 out of 47 searches. Google won about 15. And 11 were roughly tied. That's not the Perplexity landslide I expected—but it's not a Google blowout either.

Day 6-7 (Weekend): The Real-World Stress Test

Weekends are different from workdays. I'm not doing research—I'm looking up recipes, checking movie reviews, finding event times, checking weather, looking up sports scores. Basically, the kind of everyday searches that Google has spent 25 years optimizing for.

And yeah, Google crushed it. "What time does Target close" is a query Google answers in 0.3 seconds with a direct answer card. Perplexity gives you a paragraph. For "best pizza near me," Google has reviews, photos, wait times, and a map. Perplexity has a text-based list.

But I did find a few weekend wins for Perplexity. When I wanted to understand the plot of a movie I was considering watching (without spoilers), Perplexity's conversational approach was great. I could say "Give me the premise of The Brutalist without any spoilers" and get exactly that—whereas Google's results were a minefield of review headlines with varying spoiler levels.

The Verdict: What I'm Actually Going to Do

After seven days and 200+ searches, here's my honest take.

Perplexity is better than Google for:

  • Research questions that require synthesizing multiple sources
  • Complex topics where you want a clear, cited explanation
  • Follow-up questions and drilling deeper into a topic
  • Getting the answer without wading through ads and SEO content
  • Comparing products, services, or concepts side-by-side

Google is still better for:

  • Local searches (restaurants, stores, services near me)
  • Shopping and price comparisons
  • Maps and navigation
  • Image and video search
  • Real-time information (sports scores, stock prices, weather)
  • YouTube content discovery

Am I ditching Google completely? No. That would be impractical. Google's ecosystem—Maps, Shopping, YouTube, Gmail integration—is too deeply woven into how I use the internet.

But am I keeping Perplexity as my second tab? Absolutely. For maybe 40% of my daily searches—the research-heavy, complex, "I actually need to understand something" queries—Perplexity is genuinely faster and more useful than Google. And that percentage is growing as Perplexity keeps improving.

My prediction? Within two years, most people will use both an AI search tool and Google, the same way most people now use both a smartphone and a laptop. They're complementary tools for different jobs.

The era of one search engine to rule them all? I think it's ending. And honestly, that's probably a good thing for everyone.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Perplexity AI better than Google Search?

For research-heavy, multi-step questions, Perplexity AI is often better than Google because it synthesizes information from multiple sources and provides direct answers with citations. For quick local searches, shopping, maps, and navigation, Google is still superior. The best approach is using both depending on the type of query.

Is Perplexity AI free to use?

Yes, Perplexity AI has a free tier that includes unlimited Quick searches and 5 Pro searches per day. The Pro plan costs $20/month and gives you 600+ Pro searches per day, access to more powerful AI models, and file upload capabilities.

Can Perplexity AI replace Google completely?

Not yet. While Perplexity excels at answering complex questions with cited sources, it can't replace Google for local business searches, maps navigation, image search, shopping comparisons, or integration with the broader Google ecosystem. It's best used as a complement to Google, not a complete replacement.

How accurate is Perplexity AI compared to Google?

In my testing, Perplexity AI was accurate about 85% of the time with verifiable cited sources. Google's AI Overviews have similar accuracy but are harder to verify since citations aren't always clear. The key advantage of Perplexity is that you can click through to check every claim against its original source.
