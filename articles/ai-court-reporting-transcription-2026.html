AI Court Reporting and Legal Transcription

By Sarah Mitchell March 16, 2026 10 min read

A federal judge told me she was using AI transcription to create real-time trial transcripts for pro se litigants who can't afford stenographers. The accuracy is 92-95%, which isn't good enough for the official record (that still requires human court reporters), but it's good enough for litigants to understand what's actually happening in their case. That's not a technology flex — that's justice access improvement.

Court reporting and legal transcription is in the midst of a generational shift driven by AI. The shortage of court reporters is severe and worsening — the National Court Reporters Association estimates that 40% of practicing court reporters will retire within the next five years, with fewer people entering the profession. AI transcription isn't replacing court reporters wholesale, but it's creating a hybrid model where AI handles high-volume transcription, freeing human reporters for complex or high-stakes proceedings where accuracy and precision matter most.

Court Reporting and Transcription Technologies

The landscape includes specialized tools and general-purpose transcription:

  • Specialized court reporting platforms: Systems designed for depositions and courtroom use with legal-specific vocabulary and formats
  • General-purpose AI transcription: Tools like Otter.ai, Descript, and Rev that handle legal audio with varying accuracy
  • Real-time transcription: CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation) services for live courtroom and deposition use
  • Document discovery and review: AI that searches transcripts for relevant evidence and creates summaries
  • Deposition analytics: AI that flags inconsistencies in testimony across multiple depositions

Rev: The Gold Standard for Legal Transcription

Rev has become the default for attorneys needing high-accuracy transcription. At $0.25/minute (roughly $15 per recorded hour), it's not cheap, but the 99%+ accuracy makes it court-usable for critical depositions and oral arguments. Rev uses a hybrid approach: AI does initial transcription, then human editors review and correct to ensure accuracy.

For depositions, this hybrid model is essential. The slight delay (typically 24-48 hours) is acceptable for most civil litigation, where depositions are scheduled in advance and parties don't need same-day transcripts. Rev's interface integrates with many deposition platforms and practice management systems, making it a natural choice for firms already in the ecosystem.

Otter.ai for Real-Time Depositions and Hearings

Otter.ai ($180/month for Business tier) serves a different use case: real-time transcription for depositions, hearings, and meetings where attorneys need a working transcript immediately. The accuracy is 85-95% depending on audio quality and speaker clarity, which is acceptable for attorney note-taking but not for official court records.

The unique advantage is Otter's speaker identification and real-time searching. In a three-person deposition (plaintiff, defendant, attorney), Otter labels which person is speaking throughout. You can search the transcript during the deposition to find what a witness said about a specific topic 45 minutes ago, immediately confront them with it. This is a genuine tactical advantage for deposition cross-examination.

Lawyers increasingly use Otter for depositions alongside human court reporting — the Otter transcript becomes a working copy for preparation, while the official record comes from a human stenographer. For informal witness interviews or document review meetings, Otter standalone is sufficient.

Relativity and E-Discovery Transcription

For large-scale litigation involving extensive recorded interviews, interrogations, or surveillance footage, Relativity's AI transcription system is purpose-built. The platform can process thousands of hours of audio files, transcribe them at scale with 98%+ accuracy, and integrate the transcripts into Relativity's document review workflow for searchable discovery.

This is enterprise legal technology — it's accessible primarily to large law firms, corporate legal departments, and prosecutors handling significant litigation. The pricing is custom based on volume, but a typical six-month engagement might cost $50,000-$200,000 depending on total hours transcribed.

The Human Reporter vs. AI Transcription Debate

This is where the conversation gets nuanced. Human court reporters remain necessary for official trial transcripts — statutory requirement in most jurisdictions. Human reporters provide certified, verbatim records with guaranteed accuracy. They can also provide real-time realtime transcription (CART) that parties can see as it happens, essential for deaf and hard-of-hearing litigants.

The realistic picture for 2026: AI transcription handles high-volume, lower-stakes transcription (routine depositions, working transcripts, interview notes). Human reporters focus on trials, complex oral arguments, and proceedings where certified records are required. The market is bifurcating, with AI taking volume that was previously done by newer or less experienced reporters, while senior court reporters focus on higher-value, more complex work.

For law firms and courts, this hybrid model is actually economically healthy. AI handles commoditized transcription efficiently; human expertise is deployed where it genuinely adds value.

Deposition Analytics: The Emerging Layer

Beyond transcription, a new category of AI tools analyzes transcribed testimony. Platforms like Deposition Edge and features in major e-discovery systems use AI to:

  • Identify testimony inconsistencies across multiple depositions
  • Flag areas where the same question yielded different answers from different witnesses
  • Surface "important testimony" — moments when witness demeanor or response time changed dramatically
  • Create summaries of specific topics across multiple depositions

For complex litigation with dozens of depositions, this analytics layer dramatically compresses review time. Attorneys no longer read through 100 six-hour depositions manually — AI surfaces the contradictions and key moments, making review and case strategy decisions far more efficient.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can AI transcripts be used as official court records?

Not yet in most jurisdictions. Official court records still require human court reporters in most states, though some courts are piloting AI transcription for non-binding proceedings. If your transcript might become part of an official court record, use human stenographers or platforms like Rev that provide certified, human-reviewed transcripts. For working transcripts and attorney notes, AI is perfectly adequate.

What's the minimum audio quality needed for AI legal transcription?

Most platforms require at least 16-bit, 44.1kHz audio. Phone quality audio produces significantly lower accuracy (80-90%) compared to good recording equipment (95-99%). For depositions, invest in a decent digital recorder or Zoom recording if using video conferencing. The $200-400 investment in a quality microphone is worth it for 5-10% accuracy improvement.

How much faster is AI transcription than human court reporters?

AI provides same-day or next-day delivery compared to 1-2 weeks for traditional court reporting (turnaround varies). Otter.ai provides real-time transcription during the proceeding, though with lower accuracy than post-process transcription. For speed, AI wins dramatically. For accuracy and certification, human reporting still wins.

How do I choose between Rev, Otter, and other transcription services?

Use Rev if: you need 99%+ accuracy for court-usable transcripts, willing to wait 24-48 hours, need edited and certified transcripts. Use Otter if: you need real-time transcription during proceedings, speaker identification is important, willing to accept 90-95% accuracy. Use Rev for official records, Otter for working transcripts and tactical deposition support.
