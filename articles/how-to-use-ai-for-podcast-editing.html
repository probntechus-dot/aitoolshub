I started a podcast in 2023. Recorded my first episode in 40 minutes. Then spent four hours editing it. Cutting dead air. Removing the 47 times I said "um." Trying to figure out why my audio sounded like I was recording inside a metal trash can.

I almost quit after episode three. The content was the fun part — the editing was killing me.

Then a friend told me about Descript, and my whole relationship with podcasting changed. Suddenly I could edit audio by deleting words from a transcript. The AI removed my filler words automatically. Another tool fixed my room echo with one click.

Two years later, I've tested every major AI podcast tool on the market. My editing time went from 4 hours per episode to about 90 minutes — and the audio quality is better than ever. Here's exactly what I use and how I use it.

🔑 The Bottom Line

AI won't make your podcast content better — that's still on you. But it will remove the technical barriers that make podcasting feel like a second job. Good content + AI editing = a podcast you'll actually stick with.

The Editing Problem Every Podcaster Knows

If you've ever edited a podcast manually, you know the pain. Traditional editing in Audacity or GarageBand means staring at waveforms, listening to your own voice on repeat, and making hundreds of tiny cuts. It's tedious. It's time-consuming. And it's the number one reason people start podcasts and then abandon them within 10 episodes.

Here's what typically eats up your time:

  • Filler words: Um, uh, like, you know, so — they add up to minutes of dead weight per episode
  • Awkward pauses: Those 3-5 second silences while you think of what to say next
  • Audio quality issues: Room echo, background noise, inconsistent volume levels
  • Content editing: Removing tangents, reordering sections, tightening up rambling answers
  • Post-production: Adding intros, outros, music, and normalizing levels

AI tackles every single one of these. Not perfectly — I'll be honest about the limitations — but well enough to turn a 4-hour job into a 90-minute one. Let me walk you through each tool.

Descript: Edit Audio Like a Google Doc

Descript turns your audio into text — then lets you edit by simply deleting words from the transcript. Photo: Unsplash

Descript is the tool that made me fall in love with podcast editing. The core idea is brilliant: it transcribes your audio, shows you the transcript, and when you delete words from the text, it removes them from the audio too. You're editing audio by editing a document.

Let me explain why this matters. In traditional editing, you're scrubbing through a timeline, listening for the exact moment someone says "um," placing your cursor, cutting, then moving to the next one. In Descript, you see every "um" highlighted in the transcript. You hit delete. Done. What used to take 30 minutes takes 30 seconds.

What Descript Does Best

  • Text-based editing: Edit audio and video by editing the transcript text
  • Filler word removal: One click removes all ums, uhs, likes, and you-knows
  • Studio Sound: AI audio enhancement that removes echo and background noise
  • AI summaries and show notes: Generates episode descriptions and chapter markers
  • Overdub: AI voice cloning to fix small mistakes without re-recording

My Honest Take

Descript is the single best AI tool for podcast editing, full stop. The text-based editing approach is so intuitive that going back to waveform editing feels like going back to a typewriter after using a word processor. The only downside is that transcription accuracy drops a bit with heavy accents or multiple people talking at once. But for standard podcast conversations, it's 95-98% accurate.

Adobe Podcast: Free Audio Cleanup That Actually Works

Adobe Podcast has one killer feature that makes it worth knowing about: Enhance Speech. It's a free web tool — you upload your audio file, and AI cleans it up. Removes background noise. Reduces echo. Normalizes volume. And the results are honestly kind of shocking.

I tested it with a recording I made in a hotel room with the AC blasting. The original audio was genuinely bad — you could hear the air conditioning louder than my voice in some parts. After running it through Enhance Speech, it sounded like I was in a treated recording studio. I played both versions for a friend and she couldn't believe they were the same recording.

When to Use Adobe Podcast

  • You recorded in a noisy environment and need to rescue the audio
  • Your guest was on a bad microphone or in a room with echo
  • You want to clean up audio before importing it into Descript or another editor
  • You're on a tight budget and need free audio cleanup

The Limitations

It's just audio cleanup — not a full editing solution. You can't cut, rearrange, or add anything. Think of it as a pre-processing step. I often run my raw audio through Adobe Podcast first, then import the cleaned file into Descript for actual editing. That combo gives me the best results.

The other limitation is file size — there's a cap on how long your audio can be. For episodes over 60 minutes, you may need to split the file first.

Auphonic: Set It and Forget It Audio Mastering

Auphonic is the tool most professional podcasters use that most beginners have never heard of. It's an automated audio post-production service that handles leveling, noise reduction, loudness normalization, and encoding — all the technical stuff that makes your podcast sound "professional" rather than "amateur."

Here's what makes Auphonic different from the others: you set your preferences once, then just upload your files. It applies the same processing every time, so every episode has consistent quality. No tweaking sliders. No guessing about loudness standards. It just works.

What Auphonic Handles

  • Loudness normalization: Hits the -16 LUFS standard that podcast platforms want
  • Leveling: Balances volume between speakers (no more one guest being twice as loud)
  • Noise reduction: Removes consistent background noise like fans, AC, or street sounds
  • Encoding: Exports in the right format and bitrate for podcast distribution
  • Chapter markers: Can auto-generate chapter markers based on content

Riverside: Record and Edit in One Place

Riverside started as a remote recording platform — a better alternative to Zoom for podcast interviews. But in the last year, they've added AI editing features that make it a serious all-in-one solution.

The pitch is simple: record your interview on Riverside, and it automatically transcribes, removes filler words, creates clips for social media, and generates show notes. You don't have to export to another tool. Everything happens in one place.

Why Riverside Stands Out

  • Local recording quality: Records each participant's audio locally (not over the internet), so quality is always studio-grade regardless of connection speed
  • AI clips: Automatically identifies the most engaging moments and creates short clips for social media
  • Magic Editor: Text-based editing similar to Descript, built right into the recording platform
  • Video + audio: If you're doing a video podcast, Riverside handles both formats

My take: Riverside is ideal if you do a lot of remote interviews and want the simplest possible workflow. Record → AI edits → export → publish. If you're a solo podcaster or already have a recording setup you like, Descript might be a better fit since you're paying for editing power, not recording features.

My Complete AI Podcast Workflow (90 Minutes Start to Finish)

Here's my actual workflow for a typical 45-minute podcast episode. I've timed each step.

Step 1: Record (45 minutes)
I use a Rode PodMic USB and record in Riverside (for interviews) or just a simple audio recording app (for solo episodes). I don't worry about being perfect — I know AI will clean up the mistakes later. This removes a ton of performance anxiety.

Step 2: Audio cleanup — Adobe Podcast Enhance (5 minutes)
Upload the raw audio to Adobe Podcast's free Enhance tool. Download the cleaned version. This step is optional if you have a good recording environment, but I do it every time because it makes everything sound noticeably better.

Step 3: Import to Descript and auto-remove filler words (5 minutes)
Import the cleaned audio into Descript. It transcribes in about 2 minutes. Then I click "Remove filler words" and let it do its thing. Instantly removes 50+ filler words from a typical episode.

Step 4: Content editing in Descript (20-30 minutes)
This is the part that still requires my brain. I read through the transcript, delete sections that drag, tighten up transitions, and cut any tangents that don't serve the listener. But because I'm reading text instead of scrubbing a timeline, this goes 3-4x faster than traditional editing.

Step 5: Run through Auphonic for mastering (5 minutes)
Export from Descript, upload to Auphonic, download the mastered file. Levels are perfect, loudness is normalized, encoding is correct. Every single time.

Step 6: Generate show notes with AI (10 minutes)
Descript auto-generates show notes and chapter markers. I paste the transcript into ChatGPT and ask it to create a blog post version, 5 social media posts, and 3 email newsletter hooks. Takes 10 minutes to review and finalize everything.

Total editing time: ~50-55 minutes for a 45-minute episode. Compare that to 3-4 hours the old way.

💡 The 80/20 Rule of Podcast Editing

Don't aim for perfection. Aim for "good enough that no listener will notice." AI removes the obvious stuff (filler words, bad audio). Your manual editing handles the important stuff (content flow and pacing). The last 20% of polish gives diminishing returns that most audiences won't notice.

Bonus: Repurposing Your Episode With AI

One podcast episode can become 10+ pieces of content with the right AI tools. Photo: Unsplash

The biggest missed opportunity in podcasting is not repurposing your episodes. You spent 90 minutes creating a 45-minute piece of content — why would you only publish it in one place?

Here's how AI helps me turn one episode into a content machine:

  • Blog post: Feed the transcript to ChatGPT → get a 1,500-word article (edit for 15 minutes)
  • Short video clips: Riverside or Opus Clip automatically finds the best 60-second moments → post on TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts
  • Quote graphics: Pull the best quotes from the transcript → drop into Canva templates → post on X and LinkedIn
  • Newsletter: Take the top 3 insights from the episode → write a quick email blast to your list
  • YouTube video: If you recorded video, upload the full episode with AI-generated chapters

One recording session. Five to ten pieces of content. That's the magic of combining podcasting with AI repurposing tools.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the best free AI tool for podcast editing?

Descript offers the most generous free tier — you get 1 hour of transcription per month, basic editing features, and filler word removal. For audio cleanup specifically, Adobe Podcast's Enhance Speech tool is completely free and works surprisingly well even on terrible recordings.

Can AI completely replace manual podcast editing?

Not entirely, but it can handle 70-80% of the work. AI excels at removing filler words, cleaning up audio quality, generating transcripts, and creating show notes. You'll still want to manually check for context-sensitive edits, pacing, and content flow. The goal is faster editing, not zero editing.

How accurate are AI transcriptions for podcasts in 2026?

Very accurate — typically 95-98% for clear English audio. Descript and Riverside both hit this range consistently. Accuracy drops with heavy accents, multiple overlapping speakers, or poor audio quality, but it's still much faster than transcribing manually.

Is Descript worth paying for as a podcaster?

If you publish weekly or more, absolutely. The Pro plan ($24/month) pays for itself in time savings alone. The text-based editing, filler word removal, and Studio Sound features typically cut editing time from 3-4 hours to about 1 hour per episode.

Can AI help me repurpose podcast episodes into other content?

Yes, and this is one of the biggest wins. AI can turn a single podcast episode into blog posts, social media clips, email newsletters, YouTube shorts, and quote graphics. Tools like Descript, Opus Clip, and ChatGPT make repurposing almost automatic.
