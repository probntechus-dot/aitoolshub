AI Ghostwriting Tools for Authors: Write Your Book Faster in 2026

By Sarah Mitchell February 12, 2026 Writing 12 min read

Three months ago, I was staring at page 47 of a manuscript that had been stuck at page 47 for nearly five weeks. My protagonist was mid-sentence in a pivotal confrontation scene, and I simply could not figure out what she would say next. On a whim, I pasted the last two pages into Sudowrite, clicked "Write," and watched as the AI produced a paragraph that made me sit up straight in my chair. It wasn't perfect—it never is—but it cracked open a door I'd been pounding on for over a month. That single moment changed how I approach long-form writing, and it sent me down a rabbit hole of testing every AI ghostwriting tool I could get my hands on.

Over the next ten weeks, I ran six different AI writing assistants through a gauntlet of real-world fiction and nonfiction tasks: drafting chapters, expanding outlines into scenes, rewriting weak passages, and generating dialogue. I wrote more than 70,000 words across multiple projects, carefully logging which tool produced usable output and which generated the kind of generic slop that would embarrass a first-year creative writing student. Here's what I found.

1. Sudowrite — The Fiction Author's Secret Weapon

If you write fiction, stop reading and go try Sudowrite right now. I'm only half joking. This tool was built from the ground up for creative writers, and it shows in every feature. The Story Engine can take your outline, character notes, and a few sample paragraphs of your prose style, then generate entire chapters that sound remarkably like you. Not like an AI. Not like a content farm article. Like your actual writing voice.

During my test, I fed it the first three chapters of a psychological thriller I'd been developing. Story Engine generated a 2,400-word fourth chapter that captured my protagonist's sardonic inner monologue so accurately that my beta reader couldn't tell which sections were AI-assisted. I still had to rework about 30% of the output—mostly tightening metaphors and fixing a timeline inconsistency—but the bones were solid.

Pricing: Hobby plan at $19/month (billed annually at $10/month) with 225,000 AI credits; Professional at $29/month ($22/month annual) with 1,000,000 credits; Max at $59/month ($44/month annual) with 2,000,000 credits. The Professional tier is the sweet spot for most working authors.

Standout Feature: The "Describe" tool generates sensory-rich prose—sight, sound, smell, taste, touch—from a simple prompt. I used it to flesh out setting descriptions and it consistently produced vivid, non-clichéd imagery.

Limitation: Sudowrite struggles with very long narrative arcs. It can lose track of subplots introduced three or four chapters earlier, so you need to actively manage continuity through your notes.

2. NovelAI — Maximum Creative Freedom

NovelAI takes a radically different approach from Sudowrite. Where Sudowrite guides you through structured writing workflows, NovelAI hands you a blank canvas with an extraordinarily powerful autocomplete engine and says, "Go." The result feels less like using a tool and more like improvising with an unpredictable but talented co-writer.

I spent two weeks drafting a fantasy novella using NovelAI's Kayra model, and the results were fascinating. The AI produced some of the most inventive metaphors I encountered in any tool—things I genuinely wished I'd thought of myself. But it also had a tendency to veer into purple prose without warning, and it occasionally generated content that contradicted established details from earlier in the manuscript.

Pricing: Tablet plan at $10/month with 1,000 Anlas credits; Scroll plan at $15/month with 1,000 Anlas plus priority access; Opus at $25/month with 10,000 Anlas and unlimited text generation. The Opus plan is where the real value lives.

Standout Feature: Custom AI modules trained on specific genres or authors. You can train the model on your previous published works to create a bespoke writing partner that understands your style at a granular level.

Limitation: The learning curve is steep. Without the structured workflows of competitors, new users often feel overwhelmed by the sheer number of configuration options.

3. Jasper AI — The Nonfiction Powerhouse

Jasper wasn't designed for novelists, and trying to use it for fiction confirmed that quickly. But for nonfiction authors—memoirists, self-help writers, business book authors—it's a formidable drafting machine. Jasper's Brand Voice feature let me upload three chapters of a client's business book and then generate new content that matched their authoritative, data-driven tone with impressive consistency.

I used Jasper to draft a 5,000-word chapter on leadership frameworks, and approximately 65% of the output was usable with light editing. The AI excelled at structuring arguments, finding logical transitions between ideas, and generating compelling topic sentences. Where it fell short was in producing original anecdotes or insights—everything felt drawn from common knowledge rather than personal experience.

Pricing: Creator plan at $39/month per seat; Pro plan at $59/month per seat with Brand Voice, SEO mode, and additional features; Business plan with custom pricing for teams. Both Creator and Pro offer 7-day free trials.

Standout Feature: Brand Voice analyzes your existing content and creates a style profile that influences all future generations. For ghostwriters maintaining a client's voice across hundreds of pages, this is genuinely transformative.

Limitation: Jasper's output reads like polished marketing copy even when you want a conversational or literary tone. Breaking it out of that register requires significant prompt engineering.

4. Claude (Anthropic) — The Thoughtful Collaborator

I wasn't planning to include a general-purpose AI in this roundup, but Claude earned its spot through sheer quality of prose. When given a detailed scene outline, character backgrounds, and a few paragraphs of sample writing, Claude produced fiction that was surprisingly literary. Its dialogue felt natural, its pacing was deliberate, and it grasped subtext in ways that left me genuinely impressed.

The catch, of course, is that Claude wasn't designed as a dedicated writing tool. There's no manuscript management, no story bible, no chapter-by-chapter workflow. You're copying and pasting into a chat interface, which becomes tedious during sustained writing sessions. But for breaking through specific stuck points or generating high-quality first-draft material for individual scenes, Claude outperformed several dedicated writing platforms.

Pricing: Free tier with usage limits; Claude Pro at $20/month with extended context windows and priority access. The Pro tier's 200K token context window means it can hold an entire novel's worth of material in a single conversation.

Standout Feature: Exceptional understanding of nuance, tone, and subtext. Claude handles emotionally complex scenes—grief, moral ambiguity, unreliable narrators—better than any specialized writing tool I tested.

Limitation: No built-in manuscript management. You need to bring your own organizational system, which adds friction for long projects.

5. Rytr — Budget-Friendly Starting Point

Rytr occupies the budget tier of AI writing tools, and its output reflects that positioning. At $9/month for the Saver plan, you get unlimited characters and access to over 40 use cases. For authors who want to experiment with AI-assisted writing without a significant financial commitment, it's a reasonable entry point.

However, my testing revealed consistent issues. Rytr's fiction output was notably more generic than Sudowrite or NovelAI—characters felt flat, dialogue was stilted, and descriptions relied heavily on tired phrases. I had to rewrite roughly 70% of every passage it generated, which raises the question of whether the time savings justify even a modest subscription.

Pricing: Free plan with 10,000 characters/month; Saver plan at $9/month with unlimited characters; Unlimited plan at $29/month with a dedicated account manager and priority support.

Standout Feature: The sheer breadth of use cases. Need to generate a book blurb, author bio, query letter, and three chapters in one sitting? Rytr can attempt all of it from a single dashboard.

Limitation: Prose quality is noticeably below Sudowrite and NovelAI. Fine for rough brainstorming, but not for generating publishable draft material.

6. Scrivener + AI Integrations — The Organizational Backbone

Scrivener itself isn't an AI tool, but its $59.99 one-time purchase price and legendary organizational features make it the manuscript management system that every AI-assisted author needs. I used Scrivener as my central hub throughout all testing, copying AI-generated content into its binder structure, using its corkboard view to rearrange AI-drafted scenes, and leveraging its compile feature to export clean manuscripts.

The newest versions allow integration with external AI services through custom scripts and browser-based workflows. Several community-built plugins now pipe Sudowrite and Claude output directly into Scrivener documents, which eliminated the copy-paste friction I mentioned earlier.

Pricing: $59.99 one-time purchase for Mac or Windows; $50.99 for students and academics; iOS version at $23.99. No subscription fees whatsoever.

Standout Feature: The Snapshot feature lets you preserve a version of every scene before applying AI suggestions, making it trivially easy to compare before-and-after and revert changes you don't like.

Limitation: No native AI features. Every integration requires additional setup, and the learning curve for Scrivener itself is notoriously steep for new users.

The Verdict: Matching the Right Tool to Your Writing Life

After 70,000 words and ten weeks of intensive testing, my recommendation is layered rather than singular. Fiction authors should start with Sudowrite's Professional plan and pair it with Scrivener for manuscript management. Nonfiction authors will find Jasper's Brand Voice feature indispensable for maintaining consistency across long works. And for those pivotal moments when you need a genuinely thoughtful creative partner—the scene that needs emotional precision, the dialogue that needs to feel painfully real—Claude remains my go-to.

The tools aren't perfect. None of them will write your book for you, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. But they will help you get unstuck faster, draft more prolifically, and spend more of your creative energy on the high-level decisions that make a book worth reading. That's not a small thing. For a working author fighting deadlines and blank pages, it might be everything.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can AI ghostwriting tools actually write an entire novel?

No current AI tool can write a publish-ready novel from scratch. Tools like Sudowrite and NovelAI excel at generating drafts, expanding scenes, and overcoming writer's block, but every output requires significant human editing to maintain consistent voice, plot coherence, and emotional depth. Think of them as a writing partner, not a replacement.

Which AI ghostwriting tool is best for fiction authors?

Sudowrite is purpose-built for fiction and consistently produces the most natural-sounding prose. Its Story Engine feature understands narrative structure, and at $22/month for the Professional plan, it offers the best balance of quality and affordability for novelists.

Will publishers reject books written with AI assistance?

Most publishers currently have policies requiring disclosure of AI assistance. However, using AI as a brainstorming and drafting tool—similar to how authors use editors and ghostwriters—is generally accepted as long as the final work reflects your creative vision and voice. Always check submission guidelines for specific publishers.

How much faster can AI tools help me write a book?

Based on our testing, AI tools reduced first-draft completion time by 40-60%. A novel that might take 6 months to draft could be completed in 2-3 months. However, editing and revision time remains similar, as AI-generated passages need careful polishing to match your voice.

Are AI ghostwriting tools worth the monthly subscription cost?

If you're actively writing and publishing, the $10-44/month cost of tools like Sudowrite pays for itself quickly. A single published book can generate hundreds or thousands in royalties. The time savings alone—often 15-20 hours per month—make these tools a sound investment for serious authors.
