Zapier vs Make (Integromat): Which Automation Tool Is Better?

⏱ 9 min read
By Sarah Mitchell March 14, 2026
📝 Disclosure: Some links in this article are affiliate links. If you purchase through them, we may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. We only recommend tools we've personally tested.

I have a confession: I was a Zapier loyalist for years. I'd set up a Zap, watch it run, and feel like a productivity genius. Then a friend showed me her Make dashboard — these beautiful visual workflows with branching paths and conditional logic — and I realized I might have been overpaying for simplicity.

That was two years ago. Since then, I've built serious automations on both platforms. I've hit Zapier's pricing wall. I've wrestled with Make's learning curve. And I've formed some strong opinions about when each tool is the right choice.

If you're trying to decide between Zapier and Make in 2026, here's what I actually think after using both daily.

The Quick Version: Who Should Pick What

If you want the short answer before we dig in:

  • Choose Zapier if you want the easiest setup, need a specific niche app integration, or you're building simple 2-3 step automations.
  • Choose Make if you want more control, build complex multi-step workflows, or you're budget-conscious and running lots of automations.

Now let's get into the details.

Ease of Use: Zapier Wins (But It's Complicated)

Zapier's interface is almost stupidly simple, and I mean that as a compliment. You pick a trigger app, pick an action app, map a few fields, and turn it on. A first-time user can build a working automation in under five minutes. There's something beautiful about that.

Make takes a different approach. Instead of a linear step-by-step builder, you get a visual canvas. You drag modules onto the canvas and connect them with lines. It looks like a flowchart, and it works like a flowchart — complete with branches, loops, error handlers, and routers.

The first time I opened Make, I stared at the screen for ten minutes trying to figure out where to start. The canvas felt overwhelming compared to Zapier's clean list view. But once I built my first scenario (that's what Make calls workflows), I understood the appeal. You can see the logic. You can see where data flows, where it branches, where errors get caught.

For simple automations, Zapier's simplicity is unbeatable. For anything with conditional logic, Make's visual approach is genuinely better.

Pricing: Make Is Dramatically Cheaper

This is where things get interesting — and where a lot of people (including me) start reconsidering their Zapier subscription.

Plan Zapier Make
Free tier 100 tasks/month, 5 Zaps 1,000 operations/month, 2 scenarios
Starter $19.99/mo — 750 tasks $9/mo — 10,000 operations
Professional $49/mo — 2,000 tasks $16/mo — 10,000 operations
Team $69/mo — 2,000 tasks $29/mo — 10,000 operations
Enterprise Custom pricing Custom pricing

Look at those numbers. Make gives you 10,000 operations on its $9/month plan. Zapier gives you 750 tasks for $19.99. That's a staggering difference, especially if you run automations frequently.

Now, a "task" in Zapier and an "operation" in Make aren't exactly the same thing. In Zapier, each action step counts as one task. In Make, each module execution counts as one operation. A complex workflow might use more operations in Make, but even accounting for that, Make typically costs 3-5x less for the same workload.

I switched my high-volume automations to Make last year and cut my automation costs from $49/month to $16/month. Same results. Less money.

Integrations: Zapier Has More Apps

Zapier connects to over 7,000 apps. Make connects to about 1,800. That's a huge gap, and for some people, it's the deciding factor.

If you need to connect a niche CRM, an industry-specific tool, or a lesser-known SaaS app, Zapier almost certainly has it. Make covers all the popular tools — Google Workspace, Slack, Notion, Shopify, HubSpot, Airtable — but it's missing some of the long-tail integrations.

That said, Make has a powerful HTTP module that lets you connect to any app with an API, even if there's no pre-built integration. It takes more technical skill, but it's doable. Zapier has webhooks too, but Make's approach feels more natural for API work.

AI Features: Both Are Getting Smarter

Both platforms jumped on the AI trend, but they took different approaches.

Zapier's AI features:

  • Natural language workflow builder — describe what you want and Zapier suggests a Zap
  • AI-powered field mapping that auto-suggests which data goes where
  • Built-in ChatGPT and AI actions you can add to any Zap
  • AI troubleshooting that explains errors in plain English

Make's AI features:

  • Dedicated AI modules for OpenAI, Anthropic Claude, Google Gemini, and others
  • AI-powered text analysis and generation within workflows
  • Custom AI pipelines where you can chain multiple AI models together
  • Vision and audio processing modules

Zapier's AI is designed to make the platform easier to use — it helps you build automations faster. Make's AI is designed as building blocks — it gives you raw AI capability to include in your workflows. If you want AI to help you set up automations, Zapier is friendlier. If you want to build AI-powered workflows, Make gives you more control.

Complex Workflows: Make Pulls Ahead

This is where Make really separates itself. Need to build a workflow that:

  • Checks if an email contains an attachment
  • If yes, sends it to one folder; if no, sends a Slack message
  • Then waits 24 hours
  • Then sends a follow-up email
  • Unless the person already replied, in which case it creates a CRM task instead

In Zapier, you'd need multiple Zaps, some Paths (which require the Professional plan), and probably a workaround or two. In Make, it's a single scenario with routers, filters, and a sleep module. You can see the entire logic on one screen.

Make also supports loops, iterators, and aggregators — programming concepts that let you process lists of items within a single workflow. Try processing 50 line items from an invoice in Zapier, and you'll quickly feel the limitations.

Reliability and Error Handling

Both platforms are reliable in my experience. Automations run on schedule, triggers fire correctly, and downtime is rare.

Where they differ is error handling. Zapier sends you an email when a Zap fails and offers a "Replay" button to retry. It's straightforward but limited.

Make has built-in error handling routes. You can add error handler modules to any scenario that catch failures and do something useful — send a notification, retry with different parameters, log the error to a spreadsheet, or route to a fallback workflow. It's like try-catch blocks in programming, but visual.

For mission-critical automations (payment processing, client onboarding, order fulfillment), Make's error handling gives me much more confidence.

Who's Using What in 2026?

From what I've seen in communities and conversations:

  • Solopreneurs and small teams tend to start with Zapier for its simplicity, then migrate to Make when costs climb
  • Agencies and consultants often prefer Make because they can build sophisticated client workflows at lower cost
  • Enterprise teams typically choose Zapier for its governance features and larger integration library
  • Technical users almost universally prefer Make for its flexibility and visual logic

🏆 The Verdict

Pick Zapier if you want zero learning curve, need niche app integrations, or you're building simple automations and don't mind paying more for convenience. Pick Make if you want better value for money, build complex workflows with branching logic, or you're comfortable with a slightly steeper learning curve that pays off quickly. Personally? I use Make for 80% of my automations and Zapier for a handful of integrations where Make doesn't have a native connector.

My Recommendation: Start With Make

If you're starting fresh today, I'd recommend beginning with Make. Here's why:

  1. The free tier is 10x more generous. You get 1,000 operations vs. Zapier's 100 tasks. That's enough to actually test real workflows.
  2. The learning curve is worth it. Yes, it takes a day or two longer to feel comfortable. But the skills you build transfer to any automation or programming work you do later.
  3. Scaling is affordable. When your automations grow, Make scales at a fraction of Zapier's cost.
  4. The visual builder teaches you logic. You'll naturally start thinking about error handling, conditional branching, and data routing — skills that make you better at automation in general.

If Make feels too complex or doesn't have the specific integration you need, Zapier is a perfectly good Plan B. There's no wrong answer here — both tools work well. It just comes down to what you prioritize: simplicity or power.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Make really cheaper than Zapier?

Yes, significantly. Make's free plan includes 1,000 operations per month compared to Zapier's 100 tasks. Paid plans start at $9/month vs. $19.99/month. For high-volume automations, Make can cost 3-5x less.

Is Zapier easier to use than Make?

For beginners, absolutely. Zapier's step-by-step builder is intuitive enough that most people build their first automation in under five minutes. Make's visual canvas is more powerful but takes a bit longer to learn.

Can Zapier and Make connect to the same apps?

There's big overlap for popular apps (Google, Slack, Notion, Shopify, etc.), but Zapier supports 7,000+ integrations vs. Make's 1,800+. For niche tools, Zapier is more likely to have a pre-built connector.

Which tool has better AI features?

Zapier's AI helps you build automations faster with natural language. Make's AI gives you building blocks (OpenAI, Claude, Gemini modules) to create AI-powered workflows. Different strengths for different needs.

Can I switch from Zapier to Make easily?

There's no one-click migration. You'll need to rebuild workflows manually. Simple automations take 15-30 minutes to recreate; complex ones take longer but often end up more efficient on Make.
