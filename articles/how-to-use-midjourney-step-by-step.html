What Is Midjourney, Exactly?

Midjourney is an AI image generator. You type a text description (called a "prompt"), and it creates an image based on what you described. Think of it as having a very talented artist on speed dial who works in seconds instead of hours.

What makes Midjourney different from other AI image tools like DALL-E or Stable Diffusion? Two things: the aesthetic quality (Midjourney images just look better — more artistic, more polished) and the community (hundreds of thousands of creators sharing prompts and techniques).

Step 1: Setting Up Your Account (3 Minutes)

Midjourney operates through Discord (a chat platform) and through their web app. Here's how to get started:

Option A: Discord Setup (Traditional Method)

  1. Create a Discord account at discord.com if you don't have one. It's free and takes about a minute.
  2. Go to midjourney.com and click "Join the Beta" or "Sign In."
  3. Subscribe to a plan. The Basic plan ($10/month) is perfect for beginners. You get about 200 image generations, which is plenty for learning the ropes.
  4. Join the Midjourney Discord server. After subscribing, you'll get access to the server with all the image generation channels.

Option B: Web Interface (Easier for Non-Discord Users)

Midjourney now has a web interface at midjourney.com where you can generate images directly in your browser. No Discord required. It's cleaner and less overwhelming for beginners. You still need a subscription, but the generation experience is simpler.

I'd recommend starting with the web interface if Discord feels confusing, then exploring Discord later for the community features.

Step 2: Writing Your First Prompt (2 Minutes)

This is the exciting part. A "prompt" is just a text description of the image you want. You type it, Midjourney interprets it, and out comes an image (actually four variations).

In Discord, go to any #newbies channel and type:

/imagine prompt: a golden retriever puppy sitting in a field of sunflowers, warm afternoon light, photograph

On the web interface, just type the description in the prompt box — no /imagine needed.

Hit enter and wait about 30-60 seconds. Midjourney will generate four image variations. That's it — you've created your first AI image!

What Happens Next

Under your four images, you'll see buttons labeled U1, U2, U3, U4 (upscale) and V1, V2, V3, V4 (variations):

  • U buttons upscale the image — they give you a higher-resolution version of that specific image. Hit the U button for whichever of the four you like most.
  • V buttons create new variations — Midjourney generates four new images inspired by the one you picked, but with slight differences. Use this when you like the direction but want to explore alternatives.
  • 🔄 button reruns the entire prompt to get four completely new images.

Step 3: Writing Better Prompts (The Real Skill)

The difference between a mediocre Midjourney image and a jaw-dropping one is almost always the prompt. Here's how to write prompts that get great results:

The Prompt Formula

Think of your prompt in four parts:

[Subject] + [Details/Style] + [Lighting/Mood] + [Technical specifications]

Here's a basic prompt vs. a good prompt:

Basic:

a cat

Good:

a tabby cat sleeping on a stack of old books in a cozy library, dust particles floating in golden window light, oil painting style, warm earth tones, detailed fur texture

See the difference? The second prompt gives Midjourney specific information about the subject (tabby cat, sleeping), the setting (library, old books), the mood (cozy, golden light), and the style (oil painting, warm tones).

Prompt Tips That Make a Huge Difference

Be specific about what you want. "A woman" is vague. "A middle-aged woman with silver hair wearing a blue linen shirt, laughing, candid portrait" gives Midjourney something concrete to work with.

Name art styles. Adding style references dramatically changes results. Try appending these to any prompt:

  • in the style of Studio Ghibli — soft, anime-inspired, dreamy
  • photorealistic, shot on Canon EOS R5 — looks like a real photograph
  • watercolor illustration — soft, artistic, hand-painted feel
  • cyberpunk aesthetic, neon lighting — futuristic, vibrant, dramatic
  • minimalist flat vector illustration — clean, modern, graphic design style

Describe what you DON'T want. Use --no to exclude elements. For example:

a peaceful mountain lake at sunrise --no people, buildings, text

Step 4: Mastering Parameters

Parameters are the settings you add to the end of your prompt to control how Midjourney generates the image. Here are the ones you'll use most:

Aspect Ratio (--ar)

By default, Midjourney creates square images (1:1). You can change this:

a sweeping desert scene at golden hour --ar 16:9

Common aspect ratios:

  • --ar 16:9 — widescreen (great for desktop wallpapers, YouTube thumbnails)
  • --ar 9:16 — vertical (perfect for phone wallpapers, Instagram Stories, TikTok)
  • --ar 3:2 — classic photo ratio
  • --ar 2:3 — portrait orientation (Pinterest pins, book covers)

Stylize (--stylize or --s)

This controls how "artistic" Midjourney gets with your prompt. Higher values = more artistic interpretation. Lower values = closer to your literal description.

a red barn in a wheat field --s 50 (more literal)
a red barn in a wheat field --s 750 (more artistic)

The default is 100. I usually keep it between 100-300 for realistic images and crank it up to 500+ when I want something more creative and unexpected.

Chaos (--chaos or --c)

Controls how different the four image variations are from each other. Low chaos = all four look similar. High chaos = wildly different interpretations.

abstract cityscape --c 80

I use high chaos (50-100) when exploring ideas and low chaos (0-20) when I know what I want and just want slight variations to choose from.

Quality (--quality or --q)

Controls generation quality and speed. Higher quality takes longer but produces more detailed images.

  • --q .5 — fast, less detailed (good for rapid testing)
  • --q 1 — default, balanced
  • --q 2 — higher detail, uses more GPU time

Step 5: Advanced Techniques (Once You're Comfortable)

Once you've generated a few dozen images and feel comfortable with basic prompting, here are some power-user techniques:

Image References

You can upload an image and ask Midjourney to use it as a reference. This is incredibly powerful for maintaining a consistent style across multiple images. Just drag an image into Discord, copy its URL, and include it in your prompt:

/imagine prompt: [image URL] a woman walking through a rainy Tokyo street at night --iw 1.5

The --iw parameter controls how much influence the reference image has. Higher = more similar to the reference.

Style References (--sref)

Want all your images to share a consistent visual style? Use a style reference:

a cozy bookshop interior --sref [URL of an image with the style you want]

This is perfect for creating a series of images for a brand, blog, or social media account that all feel visually cohesive.

Multi-Prompts

Use double colons to give different parts of your prompt different weights:

space:: galaxy:: nebula ::2 astronaut ::1

The numbers after :: tell Midjourney how much emphasis to put on each concept. In this example, the nebula gets twice the emphasis of the astronaut.

Common Beginner Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

Writing prompts that are too short. "A dog" will give you a generic dog image. Add details. What breed? What setting? What mood? What style?

Writing prompts that are too long. Midjourney can only process so much text. Keep prompts under 60 words. If you need more detail, use parameters and image references instead of adding more words.

Forgetting to specify aspect ratio. If you need an image for a specific use (blog header, Instagram post, phone wallpaper), always set --ar. Otherwise you'll get square images that need cropping.

Not using the community. The Midjourney Discord has channels where people share their prompts alongside the images they generated. This is the fastest way to learn what works. Spend 15 minutes browsing community galleries before you start — it's incredibly inspiring.

Getting frustrated with hands and text. AI image generators have historically struggled with hands and readable text. The latest versions (v7) are much better, but it's still not perfect. If text is important, plan to add it in a graphic design tool like Canva afterward.
