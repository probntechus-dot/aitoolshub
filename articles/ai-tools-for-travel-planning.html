Plan Your Next Trip in 5 Minutes: 7 AI Travel Tools (2026)

📖 10 min read March 14, 2026 By Sarah Mitchell
🐦 Tweet 💼 LinkedIn 📘 Facebook

I used to love trip planning. I'd spend hours — sometimes days — browsing TripAdvisor reviews, cross-referencing Google Maps, reading travel blogs, building spreadsheets of flights and hotels, color-coding my itinerary by neighborhood. It was a hobby unto itself.

Then I got older. Got busier. And suddenly the 12-hour planning process for a 5-day trip started feeling less like fun and more like a second job. Sound familiar?

Here's the thing: you shouldn't have to choose between a well-planned trip and having a life. AI travel tools in 2026 can build you a detailed, personalized itinerary — with restaurant recommendations, optimal routing, budget estimates, and local tips — in the time it takes to drink a cup of coffee.

I tested seven of them by planning the same trip (10 days in Portugal) with each tool. The results ranged from "wow, this is actually better than what I'd plan myself" to "okay, this needs work." Here's what I found.

Why AI Travel Planning Actually Works Now

AI travel tools aren't new. But the early versions were basically glorified list generators — they'd spit out "Top 10 things to do in Paris" and call it an itinerary. Not helpful.

What's changed in 2026 is context awareness. These tools now understand that you can't visit a museum that's closed on Mondays, that you probably don't want to zigzag across a city, that a family with toddlers needs different recommendations than a solo backpacker, and that your budget actually matters.

The best ones also tap into real-time data — current flight prices, restaurant availability, weather forecasts, and local events. That's the difference between a generic suggestion and an itinerary you'd actually follow.

1. Layla AI — The Conversational Trip Planner

Layla AI feels like texting a friend who's been everywhere. You tell it what you want — "10 days in Portugal, mix of beach and city, mid-range budget, we love food and hate crowds" — and it builds a complete itinerary through conversation.

What makes Layla special is the back-and-forth. Don't like the hotel it suggested? Say so. Want to swap a museum day for a food tour? Just ask. It adjusts on the fly, remembering your preferences and previous requests. After 5-10 minutes of chatting, you've got a polished, day-by-day plan.

The itinerary includes estimated costs, travel time between locations, booking links, and even packing suggestions based on the weather forecast. When I planned my Portugal trip, Layla flagged that a restaurant I'd loved from a travel blog had permanently closed — saving me a wasted evening.

Pricing: Free basic plan, Premium from $9.99/trip.
Best for: Travelers who want a personalized planning conversation, not just a list.

2. Roam Around — Quick, Free, and Surprisingly Good

Roam Around is the simplest tool on this list — and that's exactly its strength. Enter your destination, how many days, and your interests. Hit generate. In about 30 seconds, you've got a day-by-day itinerary.

Is it as detailed as Layla? No. Will it miss some nuances? Probably. But if you need a solid starting point in under a minute, Roam Around delivers. I've used it as a "first draft" for trips and then refined the details manually or with another tool.

The Portugal itinerary it generated was legitimately good. It correctly grouped attractions by neighborhood, suggested reasonable time allocations, and included a mix of tourist highlights and local spots. For a completely free tool, I was impressed.

Pricing: Completely free.
Best for: Quick itinerary drafts and travelers who want something simple and fast.

3. Tripnotes — Deep Research, Beautifully Presented

Tripnotes takes a more editorial approach. Instead of giving you a bare-bones itinerary, it creates something that reads like a personalized travel magazine article — with context about why you should visit each place, what to expect, and insider tips.

For my Portugal trip, Tripnotes didn't just say "Visit Belém Tower." It explained the history, suggested the best time to go to avoid lines, recommended a nearby pastéis de nata shop, and noted that the nearby MAAT museum is a better fit for modern art lovers. That kind of layered recommendation shows genuine depth.

The downside? It's slower than other tools — generating a full itinerary takes a few minutes rather than seconds. But if you value quality over speed, the wait is worth it.

Pricing: Free tier (limited), Premium from $7.99/month.
Best for: Travelers who want rich, context-heavy recommendations — not just a list of places.

4. GuideGeek — Your WhatsApp Travel Buddy

GuideGeek takes a clever distribution approach — instead of building another app you'll download and forget, it lives inside WhatsApp (and other messaging platforms). Just text your travel questions and get instant, AI-powered answers.

"What's the best neighborhood to stay in Lisbon for first-timers?" "Is the Algarve worth visiting in March?" "How do I get from Porto to the Douro Valley without a car?" GuideGeek handles all of these with thoughtful, detailed responses.

It's not a full itinerary builder — think of it more like having a knowledgeable travel advisor in your pocket. I found it most useful during a trip, not before it. Quick questions about restaurant recommendations, transportation options, or "what should I do with a free afternoon in Porto?" are where GuideGeek shines.

Pricing: Completely free.
Best for: On-the-go travel advice and quick questions via messaging apps.

5. Wanderlog — The Planner's Planner

If you're the type of person who loves planning (but just wants to do it faster), Wanderlog is your tool. It combines AI itinerary generation with a full-featured trip planning workspace — maps, bookmarks, collaboration, budget tracking, and offline access.

The AI suggests an itinerary, and then you can drag and drop to rearrange, add your own finds, pin locations on a map, and see driving/walking times between spots. It's the perfect blend of AI speed and human control.

What I loved most was the map view. Seeing all your planned stops plotted geographically makes it obvious when your itinerary has you criss-crossing a city unnecessarily. Wanderlog's AI optimizes for this automatically, but having the visual confirmation is reassuring.

The collaboration features are great for group trips, too. Everyone can add suggestions, vote on activities, and see the final plan in one place. No more group chat chaos.

Pricing: Free tier (very generous), Pro at $4.99/month.
Best for: Detail-oriented planners and group trips where multiple people want input.

6. Google Travel AI — The Silent Powerhouse

Google doesn't market a standalone "Google Travel AI" product, but the AI features baked into Google Search, Google Maps, Google Flights, and Google Hotels have quietly become one of the best travel planning experiences available.

Search "plan a trip to Portugal" and Google now serves an AI-generated overview with suggested itineraries, flight prices, hotel options, and "things to know before you go." Google Flights' price tracking and flexible date features use AI to find the cheapest travel windows. Google Maps' AI recommendations surface restaurants and attractions based on your past preferences and reviews.

The advantage? You're probably already using these tools. There's nothing new to sign up for, no new app to learn. The AI just makes the tools you already know work smarter.

The disadvantage? It's not a unified experience. You're bouncing between Search, Maps, Flights, and Hotels — and the AI doesn't maintain context across them. You can't say "I'm the person who just searched for flights to Lisbon, now show me hotels near where I'm flying in."

Pricing: Completely free.
Best for: People who want AI-enhanced planning within tools they already use.

7. Kayak AI — Flight and Hotel Search, Reimagined

Kayak has been a travel search engine for years. But the AI features they've added recently make it feel like a completely different product.

Ask Kayak AI natural language questions: "Cheapest beach destination from New York in April for under $800 round-trip" or "Find me a hotel in Lisbon with a pool for under $150/night." It searches across hundreds of providers and gives you actual, bookable results — not just suggestions.

This is the key difference from pure planning tools. Layla and Roam Around tell you where to go. Kayak AI tells you where to go and lets you book it right there. For travelers who want to go from "I want a vacation" to "it's booked" in one session, Kayak AI is hard to beat.

I especially liked the "Explore" feature, where you can set a budget and departure city and Kayak AI suggests destinations you can afford. It's great for "I have $1,500 and a week off — surprise me" moments.

Pricing: Completely free (Kayak earns from bookings).
Best for: Travelers who want to search, compare, and book flights and hotels with conversational AI.

Which AI Travel Tool Should You Use?

After testing all seven with the same trip, here's my take:

  • Best overall experience: Layla AI — the conversational approach is addictive
  • Best for quick planning: Roam Around — 30 seconds to a solid itinerary
  • Best for detailed recommendations: Tripnotes — rich, magazine-quality suggestions
  • Best for on-the-go advice: GuideGeek — travel tips in your messaging app
  • Best for control freaks (affectionately): Wanderlog — plan, map, collaborate, obsess
  • Best for booking: Kayak AI — from idea to booked in one session
  • Best for passive planning: Google Travel AI — it improves tools you already use

Tips for Getting the Best Results from AI Travel Planners

No matter which tool you pick, these tips will get you better itineraries:

Be specific about what you like and hate. "I want to visit Italy" gives you generic results. "I want 7 days in southern Italy, I love cooking classes and hate tourist traps, moderate budget, traveling with my partner who's vegetarian" gives you magic.

Always verify opening hours and reservations. AI tools pull from databases that can be outdated. A quick Google search to confirm that a museum is open on your planned day takes 10 seconds and can save your afternoon.

Use multiple tools together. I often generate with Roam Around or Layla, refine in Wanderlog, and book through Kayak. Each tool has strengths — combine them.

Don't over-plan. The best trips have breathing room. If your AI itinerary has activities from 8 AM to 10 PM every day, cut 30% of it. Leave room for the random cafe, the unexpected detour, the "let's just wander" afternoon that becomes your favorite memory.

The Bottom Line

Trip planning used to be a spectrum: spend hours doing it yourself (good results, huge time investment) or wing it (no time investment, hit-or-miss results). AI travel tools give you a third option — personalized, detailed itineraries in minutes.

Are they perfect? No. You'll still want to verify details, add your own discoveries, and leave room for spontaneity. But as a starting point — the 80% of planning that used to take 80% of the time — these tools are remarkably good.

My honest advice: try Layla AI or Roam Around for your next trip. It'll take five minutes. If the itinerary is even 70% what you would've planned yourself, you just saved hours. And you can spend those hours doing something better — like actually being on vacation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best AI travel planning tool in 2026?

Layla AI wins for the overall experience — its conversational approach feels natural and the itineraries are detailed. But the "best" depends on your style. Wanderlog is better for planners who want control, and Kayak AI is best if you want to book everything in one place.

Are AI travel planners free?

Several are completely free — Roam Around, GuideGeek, Google Travel AI, and Kayak AI don't charge anything. Layla AI and Tripnotes have free tiers with premium options, and Wanderlog's free plan is generous enough for most trips.

Can AI travel tools book flights and hotels?

Kayak AI and Layla AI can handle bookings directly. Most others focus on planning and link out to booking platforms. Google Travel AI connects seamlessly to Google Flights and Hotels. For the best experience, I'd recommend planning with one tool and booking through Kayak or Google.

How accurate are AI-generated travel itineraries?

They're quite good for major destinations — usually 85-90% accurate. But they can miss things like seasonal closures, recently changed hours, or reservation-only restaurants. Always do a quick verification of the key details before your trip. Think of the AI itinerary as an excellent first draft, not a final document.

Do AI travel tools work for international trips?

Yes, and they're especially helpful for international trips where you're less familiar with the destination. They'll flag visa requirements, suggest local transportation options, recommend currency exchange tips, and note cultural customs. Coverage is strongest for popular tourist destinations and thinner for very remote areas.
