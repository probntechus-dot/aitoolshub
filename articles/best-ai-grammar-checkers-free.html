The 6 Best Free Grammar Checkers (AI-Powered) — Grammarly vs the Rest

I write roughly 3,000 words a day. Blog posts, emails, social media captions, client reports — it adds up. And no matter how carefully I proofread, something always slips through. A missing comma here, a repeated word there, a sentence so long it should probably be classified as a paragraph.

That's why I've been testing grammar checkers obsessively for the past two years. Not the premium versions with all the bells and whistles — the free tiers. Because if you're a student, blogger, freelancer, or just someone who sends a lot of emails, you shouldn't have to pay $30/month to avoid embarrassing typos.

I ran the same piece of intentionally error-filled text through all six tools. I tracked what each one caught, what it missed, and where it gave bad advice. Here's what I found.

1. Grammarly (Free Tier) — The One Everyone Knows

Let's get the obvious one out of the way. Grammarly has become so ubiquitous that its little green circle is practically part of the internet's furniture. And there's a reason for that: even the free version is genuinely good.

The free tier catches spelling errors, basic grammar mistakes (subject-verb agreement, article usage, tense consistency), and straightforward punctuation problems. In my test, it caught 17 out of 20 intentional errors, which is a solid hit rate for a free tool.

Where free Grammarly falls short: it won't help much with style. Wordy sentences, passive voice, unclear phrasing — those corrections are locked behind the Premium paywall ($30/month or $144/year). The free version will tell you that your sentence is grammatically correct, but it won't tell you that it's confusing or that you've used "actually" five times in two paragraphs.

The browser extension works everywhere — Gmail, Google Docs, LinkedIn, Twitter, even the text box you're typing a review in on Amazon. That alone makes it the default choice for most people.

What it catches for free: Spelling, grammar, punctuation basics.
What it misses: Style, tone, wordiness, vocabulary suggestions.
Works with: Browser extension, desktop app, mobile keyboard, Google Docs, Microsoft Office.
Best for: Anyone who wants reliable, set-it-and-forget-it grammar checking.

2. LanguageTool — The Multilingual Secret Weapon

LanguageTool is what I recommend to anyone who writes in more than one language. It supports over 30 languages — not as an afterthought, but as a core feature. The English grammar checking is excellent, and you get the same quality for Spanish, German, French, Portuguese, and dozens more.

But here's what surprised me: even for English-only writers, LanguageTool's free tier is more generous than Grammarly's in some ways. It catches more style issues for free, including some wordiness and redundancy flags that Grammarly reserves for paid users.

In my error test, LanguageTool caught 16 out of 20 errors — slightly behind Grammarly — but it also caught two style issues that Grammarly's free version ignored entirely. One was a classic dangling modifier that could have changed the meaning of a sentence.

The open-source version (which you can self-host) is completely free with no limits. The cloud version's free tier limits you to 10,000 characters per check, which is roughly 1,500-2,000 words. For most blog posts and emails, that's plenty.

What it catches for free: Grammar, spelling, some style issues, punctuation.
What it misses: Some advanced English idiom corrections.
Works with: Browser extension, Google Docs, LibreOffice, Microsoft Word, standalone app.
Best for: Multilingual writers, privacy-conscious users (open-source option), people who want more free features than Grammarly offers.

3. QuillBot — Grammar Checking Meets Paraphrasing

QuillBot started as a paraphrasing tool and added grammar checking later, but that combination turns out to be surprisingly useful. Sometimes the best fix for a bad sentence isn't correcting its grammar — it's rewriting it entirely. QuillBot lets you do both.

The grammar checker itself is solid if not spectacular. It caught 14 out of my 20 test errors, which puts it behind Grammarly and LanguageTool. Where it struggled: complex comma rules and some subject-verb agreement issues in longer sentences.

But the paraphraser is genuinely impressive. You can highlight a clunky paragraph and ask QuillBot to rewrite it in different modes — Standard, Fluency, Formal, Simple, Creative, and more. The "Fluency" mode is particularly useful for non-native English speakers who want their writing to sound more natural.

The free tier limits you to 125 words per paraphrase and includes ads, but the grammar checking is unlimited. If you mostly need grammar help and occasionally want to rephrase something, free QuillBot covers both needs.

What it catches for free: Common grammar errors, spelling, basic punctuation.
Bonus feature: Paraphrasing tool with multiple rewriting modes.
Works with: Web app, browser extension, Google Docs, Microsoft Word.
Best for: Students, non-native speakers, anyone who needs help rephrasing awkward sentences.

4. Hemingway Editor — The One That Makes You a Better Writer

Hemingway is different from everything else on this list, and I almost didn't include it because it technically isn't a grammar checker. It's a readability editor. But I include it because it does something no other free tool does nearly as well: it makes your writing clearer.

Paste your text into Hemingway and it highlights problems with color codes. Yellow for hard-to-read sentences. Red for very hard-to-read sentences. Purple for when you used a complex word and there's a simpler alternative. Blue for adverbs (which Ernest Hemingway would have told you to cut). Green for passive voice.

It doesn't catch typos or fix your comma placement. That's not its job. Its job is to take a grade-14 reading level mess and help you turn it into a grade-8 reading level piece that people actually want to read.

I use Hemingway after running text through Grammarly or LanguageTool. First, fix the errors. Then, fix the writing. The free web version does everything you need — the paid desktop app ($19.99 one-time) just adds offline access and direct publishing features.

What it catches for free: Hard-to-read sentences, passive voice, adverb overuse, complex words, readability grade.
What it doesn't do: Grammar checking, spelling, punctuation.
Works with: Free web app at hemingwayapp.com.
Best for: Bloggers, content writers, anyone whose writing tends to be dense or academic.

5. ProWritingAid — The Deep-Dive Editor for Serious Writers

ProWritingAid is the tool that book authors and professional writers quietly swear by. Its free version is the most analysis-heavy option on this list — it doesn't just tell you something is wrong, it explains why and shows you patterns in your writing.

In the free web editor (limited to 500 words per check), you get access to over 20 different writing reports. Overused words. Sentence length variation. Dialogue tag distribution. Sticky sentence percentage. If those terms sound unfamiliar, ProWritingAid explains each one and shows you how to improve.

The 500-word limit is restrictive for the free tier, no question about it. You're essentially getting a taste of what the premium version offers ($30/month or $120/year). But for short-form writing — emails, social media posts, introduction paragraphs — it's incredibly detailed.

For actual grammar checking, it caught 15 out of 20 errors in my test. Respectable, but not best-in-class. Where ProWritingAid really shines is in the style and structure analysis that goes way beyond what any other free tool provides.

What it catches for free: Grammar, style analysis, overused words, readability, sentence structure (500-word limit).
What it misses: Limited by word count; some grammar edge cases.
Works with: Web editor, browser extension (limited), Google Docs, Scrivener.
Best for: Fiction writers, long-form content creators, anyone who wants detailed writing analysis.

6. Ginger — The Underdog with a Text Reader

Ginger has been around since 2007, making it one of the oldest AI grammar tools still active. It doesn't get the press that Grammarly does, but it has a few unique features worth knowing about.

The most distinctive one: a text reader that reads your corrected text aloud. This is incredibly useful for catching errors that your eyes miss but your ears catch — awkward phrasing, missing words, sentences that just don't flow right. It's also helpful for people with dyslexia or visual processing challenges.

The grammar checking itself is decent but not outstanding. It caught 13 out of 20 errors in my test, putting it at the bottom of this list for raw accuracy. It was particularly weak on comma splices and had a tendency to miss errors in longer, complex sentences.

Where Ginger adds value beyond grammar: the translation feature (supports 40+ languages) and the personal dictionary that learns your writing patterns over time. The free tier is limited to a certain number of corrections per day, which can be frustrating if you write a lot.

What it catches for free: Basic grammar, spelling, some punctuation (daily limit).
Bonus features: Text reader, translation, personal trainer (writing exercises).
Works with: Browser extension, desktop app, iOS/Android keyboards.
Best for: Non-native English speakers, people who benefit from hearing text read aloud, language learners.

So Which One Should You Use?

After all that testing, here's my honest recommendation:

  • For most people: Start with Grammarly free. It's reliable, works everywhere, and catches the errors that matter most.
  • For multilingual writers: LanguageTool is the clear winner, especially if you write in languages other than English.
  • For students and non-native speakers: QuillBot's grammar checking + paraphrasing combo is hard to beat.
  • For bloggers and content writers: Grammarly (for errors) + Hemingway (for clarity) is the best free combo I've found.
  • For serious/fiction writers: ProWritingAid's analysis reports are worth dealing with the word limit.
  • For language learners: Ginger's text reader and translation features are uniquely helpful.

And here's a tip most articles won't tell you: use two tools together. Run your text through Grammarly first to catch errors, then paste it into Hemingway to improve readability. The whole process takes about 30 seconds and catches far more issues than either tool alone.

Your writing doesn't have to be perfect. But it does have to be clear. These tools help you get there without spending a penny.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best free grammar checker in 2026?

Grammarly's free tier remains the most well-rounded option for English writers, catching spelling, grammar, and basic punctuation errors reliably. However, LanguageTool is better for multilingual users (supporting 30+ languages), and Hemingway is better for improving readability specifically.

Is Grammarly free version good enough?

For basic grammar, spelling, and punctuation checking, the free version of Grammarly is quite capable. It misses some style and clarity suggestions reserved for Premium, but it catches the errors that matter most — the ones that make you look unprofessional.

Which grammar checker is best for non-native English speakers?

LanguageTool is the best choice for non-native speakers because it supports over 30 languages and can check grammar in your native language too. Ginger also offers translation features and a text reader that helps with pronunciation.

Are AI grammar checkers accurate?

Modern AI grammar checkers are impressively accurate for common errors. In our testing, Grammarly and LanguageTool both caught over 85% of intentional errors. They occasionally miss context-dependent mistakes or suggest changes that alter meaning, so always review suggestions before accepting them.
