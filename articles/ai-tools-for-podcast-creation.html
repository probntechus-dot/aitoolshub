Two years ago, I wanted to start a podcast. I researched microphones, audio interfaces, soundproofing foam, DAW software, hosting platforms, and RSS feeds. After three weeks of research and about $600 in equipment purchases, I recorded one episode, hated how it sounded, and quit.

Last month, I tried again. This time, I sat at my kitchen table with my laptop, opened a browser tab, and published my first episode in about two hours. No microphone. No soundproofing. No audio engineering degree. Just AI tools doing the heavy lifting.

The difference between 2024 and 2026 podcasting is staggering. The tools available now can record studio-quality audio through your laptop mic, remove background noise automatically, edit your episodes by editing text (seriously), generate show notes, and even repurpose your episodes into blog posts and social clips.

Here are the 8 tools that made it happen.

🔑 What You'll Actually Need

A laptop with a decent built-in mic (any MacBook or quality Windows laptop from the last 3 years), a quiet-ish room (doesn't need to be silent — AI handles the rest), and about $30-50/month in tool subscriptions. That's it. Everything else is optional.

The New Reality of Podcasting in 2026

The podcasting industry used to have a high barrier to entry. Good audio quality required good equipment, and good editing required technical skills. Both of those barriers are basically gone now.

AI noise removal has gotten so good that it can make a laptop mic in a noisy apartment sound like a condenser mic in a treated room. AI editing lets you cut sections by deleting text instead of scrubbing through waveforms. And AI transcription is accurate enough that you can repurpose every episode into written content automatically.

The only thing AI can't do for you is be interesting. You still need good ideas and the willingness to talk about them. But everything around that — the technical production — is solved.

1. Descript — Best All-in-One Editor

What it does: Descript is the tool that made me fall in love with podcast editing. It transcribes your audio, then lets you edit the audio by editing the transcript. Want to cut that 45-second tangent about your cat? Just highlight those sentences and delete. The audio cuts match perfectly.

What impressed me: The "Studio Sound" feature is borderline magic. I recorded a test episode using my MacBook's built-in microphone in my kitchen (dishwasher running, dog barking outside). Studio Sound removed every bit of noise and made my voice sound like I was in a professional booth. I A/B tested it against my old $300 mic setup, and honestly? It was close.

What could be better: The free tier only gives you 1 hour of transcription per month. If you're doing weekly episodes, you'll need the $24/month plan pretty quickly.

Editing audio by editing text sounds too good to be true. Descript makes it real. Photo: Kelly Sikkema on Unsplash

Descript⭐ 4.9/5
Free (1hr/mo) / $24/month Pro

If you buy only one podcasting tool, make it this one. Text-based editing is a workflow shift you won't come back from.

2. Riverside — Best for Remote Recording

What it does: Riverside records each participant's audio and video locally, then uploads the high-quality files after the session. This means even if someone's internet stutters mid-conversation, the recording is clean because it was captured locally on their device.

What impressed me: I did a test interview with a friend who was on hotel WiFi in Bangkok. On Zoom, the call would have been choppy mess. On Riverside, the final recording was crystal clear because his audio was captured by his laptop and uploaded after we finished. The separate tracks also make editing way easier — you can adjust each person's volume independently.

What could be better: The free tier is limited to 2 hours of recording per month and 720p video. For audio-only podcasts, that might be enough. For video podcasts, you'll want the $15/month plan for 1080p.

Riverside⭐ 4.7/5
Free (2hr/mo) / $15/month Standard

The gold standard for remote podcast interviews. Local recording solves every internet-quality problem.

3. Podcastle — Best for Beginners

What it does: Podcastle is built for people who have never made a podcast before. It handles recording, editing, transcription, and even has an AI that can clone your voice (with your permission) for corrections. The interface is clean, simple, and doesn't overwhelm you with audio engineering terms.

What impressed me: The "Magic Dust" audio enhancement feature is similar to Descript's Studio Sound, and it works surprisingly well. But what really sold me was the restyle feature — it can take a casual recording and add intro/outro music, normalize levels, and add subtle room tone automatically. For beginners, this removes the scariest part of production.

What could be better: The AI voice cloning for corrections sounds about 85% natural. It's good enough for fixing a mispronounced name, but not good enough for re-recording whole paragraphs. Use it sparingly.

Podcastle⭐ 4.5/5
Free (3hr/mo) / $12/month Creator

The friendliest onramp to podcasting. If technical tools intimidate you, start here.

4. Adobe Podcast — Best Audio Enhancement

What it does: Adobe Podcast's standout feature is "Enhance Speech" — an AI model trained on professional broadcast audio. You upload any audio file, and it removes noise, reduces echo, and boosts vocal clarity. It's available as a free web tool, no Creative Cloud subscription required.

What impressed me: I recorded myself talking on a busy street corner. Cars passing, people chatting, construction noise in the background. Adobe Podcast's enhancement stripped all of it out and left just my voice, clear as if I'd been in a studio. I've tested every noise removal tool on the market, and this one wins.

What could be better: It's primarily an enhancement tool, not a full production suite. You can't record or edit within it — you'll need to pair it with Descript or another editor for a complete workflow.

Adobe's AI can isolate your voice from almost any background noise. The results are genuinely impressive. Photo: Caught In Joy on Unsplash

Adobe Podcast⭐ 4.6/5
Free (Enhance Speech) / Part of Creative Cloud

The best audio enhancement AI on the market. Use it alongside your editor of choice for studio-quality output.

5. Auphonic — Best for Auto-Mastering

What it does: Auphonic takes your finished podcast episode and masters it automatically. It normalizes loudness to broadcast standards (-16 LUFS for podcasts), equalizes levels between speakers, reduces background noise, and adds metadata. Think of it as the final polish before publishing.

What impressed me: The loudness normalization alone is worth using Auphonic. Nothing is more annoying than a podcast where one speaker is whisper-quiet and the other blows out your earbuds. Auphonic fixes this automatically, and the results sound natural — not over-compressed or robotic.

What could be better: The free tier gives you 2 hours per month. For a weekly 30-minute podcast, that's perfect. For longer shows or multiple podcasts, you'll need the paid plan.

Auphonic⭐ 4.5/5
Free (2hr/mo) / $11/month for 9 hours

The "make it sound professional" button. Essential last step before you hit publish.

6. Cleanvoice — Best Filler Word Removal

What it does: Cleanvoice has one job: remove "um," "uh," "you know," "like," and other filler words from your recordings. It also removes awkward silences and mouth clicks. You upload your file, it processes it, and you get back a cleaner version.

What impressed me: I'm a chronic "um" person. In a 30-minute test recording, Cleanvoice found and removed 147 filler words. One hundred and forty-seven. Listening to the before and after was honestly a little embarrassing, but the cleaned version sounded dramatically more professional and confident.

What could be better: Occasionally it removes a deliberate pause that adds emphasis or comedic timing. You can review each edit before accepting, but that adds time. For most episodes, I just accept all and move on.

Cleanvoice⭐ 4.4/5
Pay-per-use (~$0.50/episode) or $10/month

If filler words are your nemesis (they're mine), Cleanvoice is a must-have. The pay-per-use model is perfect for occasional podcasters.

7. Resound — Best for Show Notes

What it does: Resound listens to your episode and generates show notes, chapter markers, timestamps, pull quotes, and episode summaries. It also suggests titles and creates shareable audiograms (short audio clips with waveform animations for social media).

What impressed me: The chapter markers are incredibly useful for listener experience. Resound analyzed a 45-minute interview I did and created 8 perfectly-timed chapters with descriptive titles. This used to take me 20 minutes of manual work per episode.

What could be better: The AI-generated show notes sometimes need light editing — they're about 85% ready to publish as-is. Not bad, but not set-and-forget either.

Resound⭐ 4.3/5
Free tier available / $19/month Pro

Automates the most tedious part of podcast publishing: writing show notes and creating chapters.

8. Swell AI — Best for Repurposing Content

What it does: Swell AI takes your podcast episode and turns it into a blog post, newsletter, social media posts, and video clips. It doesn't just transcribe — it restructures the content into formats that make sense for each platform.

What impressed me: I uploaded a 35-minute episode about small business marketing and Swell AI produced: a 1,200-word blog post (genuinely well-written), 5 Twitter/X threads, 3 LinkedIn posts, an email newsletter draft, and a list of suggested video clip timestamps. The blog post needed maybe 10 minutes of editing before it was publishable. That's content repurposing that would normally take a VA 3-4 hours.

Swell AI⭐ 4.4/5
Starting at $19/month

Turns one podcast episode into a week's worth of content. The ROI on content creation time is massive.

  • Editing: Descript ($24/mo) — text-based editing is a must
  • Enhancement: Adobe Podcast (free) — run audio through after editing
  • Mastering: Auphonic (free tier) — final loudness normalization
  • Repurposing: Swell AI ($19/mo) — optional but huge time saver

Total: $39-58/month depending on tiers. Compare that to the $200-500/month people spend on equipment, software, and editing services.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I really start a podcast without buying a microphone?

Yes — tools like Adobe Podcast and Descript can enhance laptop microphone audio to near-studio quality using AI noise removal and voice enhancement. That said, a $50-70 USB microphone (like the Audio-Technica ATR2100x) will still sound noticeably better. AI can fix bad audio, but it can't create quality from nothing. Start with your laptop mic and upgrade when you're committed.

What's the cheapest way to launch a podcast using AI tools?

The cheapest functional setup: record with Riverside's free tier (up to 2 hours/month), edit with Descript's free plan (1 hour of transcription/month), clean audio with Cleanvoice's pay-per-use model (~$0.50 per episode), and host on Spotify for Podcasters (free). Total cost: under $5/month for a weekly podcast.

How long does it take to produce a podcast episode with AI tools?

With a traditional workflow, producing a 30-minute episode takes 3-5 hours (recording, editing, mixing, writing show notes, publishing). With AI tools handling editing, transcription, and show notes, that drops to about 1-1.5 hours. The biggest time saver is AI-powered editing — what used to take 2 hours of cutting and splicing now takes 15-20 minutes.

Do AI podcast tools work for interview-style shows?

Absolutely. Riverside and Podcastle are specifically built for remote interviews, recording each speaker on a separate track for clean editing. Descript can identify and label different speakers automatically, making multi-person editing much easier. Cleanvoice and Auphonic handle the audio balancing between speakers who have different mic setups.

The barrier to starting a podcast has never been lower. Two years ago, it took me $600 and three weeks of research just to feel ready. Today, you can go from "I have an idea for a show" to "my first episode is live" in a single afternoon. The tools are that good. The only question left is: what do you want to talk about?
